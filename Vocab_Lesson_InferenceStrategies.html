Vocabulary Inference Strategies

How to work out the meaning of a word you have never seen — using only the text around it.
🎯 ISA Priority — Vocabulary in Context
Section 1 of 4

💡 Why You Need This Skill

In every ISA reading test, there will be words you have not seen before. You cannot look them up — so you have to infer the meaning from the text. Inferring means making a smart guess that is supported by evidence from the passage.

The Core Principle

You do not need to know a word to understand what it means. The sentence around it almost always gives you enough information to work it out.

There are four strategies for finding that information. Each strategy looks at a different kind of clue in the text.

1
Definition Clue

The sentence defines the word directly

2
Example Clue

Examples given tell you the category

3
Contrast Clue

A contrast tells you the opposite

4
General Sense

The overall mood of the sentence gives a positive or negative direction

Section 2 of 4

📋 The Four Strategies in Detail

Strategy 1

Definition Clue

The author explains the word in the same sentence or the next one. Look for: is, means, refers to, that is, in other words, or dashes (—).

Signal words: "is defined as", "means", "—", "(i.e."
Strategy 2

Example Clue

The author gives examples of the word. If you know what the examples are, you can work out the larger category they belong to.

Signal words: "such as", "for example", "including", "like"
Strategy 3

Contrast Clue

The sentence gives you the opposite meaning. If you know the opposite, you know what the word means — just flip it.

Signal words: "but", "however", "although", "unlike", "despite", "whereas"
Strategy 4

General Sense Clue

No direct clue — but the whole sentence feels positive or negative. What meaning would fit without making the sentence strange?

Ask: does this make sense if positive? If negative? Then narrow down.
Section 3 of 4

📝 Strategies in Action

Watch how each strategy is applied. The target word is highlighted in dark. The clue is highlighted in amber.

Strategy 1 — Definition Clue

A naturalist — a scientist who studies living things in their natural habitats — spent three years documenting the wildlife of the Amazon.
🔎 The dashes (—) signal a definition is coming. "Naturalist" = a scientist who studies living things in their natural habitats.

Strategy 2 — Example Clue

The museum displayed many artefacts, such as ancient pottery, carved stone tools, and bronze jewellery.
🔎 "Such as" introduces examples. All examples are old objects made by humans. "Artefacts" = objects made by people in the past, often of historical importance.

Strategy 3 — Contrast Clue

Despite her timid appearance, the young scientist delivered her speech with quiet conviction.
🔎 "Despite" signals a contrast. Her appearance was timid (shy, nervous-looking) — but she spoke with conviction. "Conviction" here = strong, confident belief. The contrast between shy appearance and confident speaking reveals the meaning.
This was a real ISA-style question

This is exactly what question Q19 from Unit 2 tested. The word "timid" had a contrast clue: "despite her timid appearance, she delivered with conviction." "Despite" tells you timid = the OPPOSITE of conviction (confident). So timid = shy, not confident.

Strategy 4 — General Sense

The explorer was undeterred by the harsh conditions — she continued climbing through three days of storms without turning back.
🔎 No direct definition or contrast clue. But the sentence tells you: harsh conditions → she continued anyway, did not stop. "Undeterred" = not stopped or discouraged by obstacles. The action tells you the meaning.
Full Worked Example — Step by Step
The archaeologist was meticulous in her work: she spent six hours on a single square metre of soil, brushing away dirt grain by grain to ensure that nothing was missed.

What does meticulous most likely mean?
1
Is there a definition, example, or contrast clue? No direct definition. No "such as" or "for example". No contrast word.
2
So I use General Sense. What does the sentence tell me she DID? She spent six hours on ONE square metre. She brushed grain by grain. She wanted nothing missed.
3
What kind of person does this? Someone who pays extremely close attention to detail. Someone who is very careful and thorough.
4
So "meticulous" = extremely careful and thorough, paying close attention to every detail.
✓ Meticulous = extremely careful and thorough, missing no detail.

🎉 All 5 Questions Complete!

You now have four reliable strategies for any unfamiliar word in the ISA.

Strategy 1 — Definition Clue

  • Signals: dashes (—), "means", "is defined as"
  • The sentence states the meaning directly
  • This is the easiest — just find the definition

Strategy 2 — Example Clue

  • Signals: "such as", "including", "for example"
  • Identify what the examples have in common
  • That shared category is the word's meaning

Strategy 3 — Contrast Clue

  • Signals: "but", "however", "unlike", "although", "despite"
  • Find what the contrast describes, then flip it
  • The target word = the opposite of the contrast

Strategy 4 — General Sense

  • No direct clue in the sentence
  • Read the whole sentence — what is happening?
  • Does positive or negative meaning fit better?
