Why this matters 🌍

On a real competition paper, you were asked what "paramount" means — and it happened to be the single highest-value question in the whole vocabulary section. The answer was "most important," but the option you picked, "negligible," actually means the exact opposite: "so small it barely matters." This is a pattern worth naming: on the HARDEST or highest-value word in a section, one of the wrong answers is often the near-OPPOSITE of the right one, not just a random wrong guess. Today you learn one extra habit that catches this specific trap.

Before We Start — quick check 🔍

Quick recap of your usual method: quote the clue, then check your answer against it.

1. "The chef was known for being extremely fastidious, arranging every plate with exact precision before it left the kitchen." What does "fastidious" mean?

Good — the quote-the-clue habit is still solid. Today we add ONE more check on top of it. Next →

Your method, plus one new step 🌳

Same method as always — find the clue, check which option the clue actually supports. Today's addition is Step 3, right at the end, just for the hardest word in a section.

Read the word in its sentence
↓
STEP 1 — Find the CLUE (the part of the sentence that describes or explains the word)
↓
STEP 2 — Which option does the clue's meaning actually CONTAIN? Quote it in your head, don't just go by "the general feeling" of the story
↓
STEP 3 (NEW) — Before locking it in, glance at the OTHER options: is one of them the near-OPPOSITE of your answer? If yes, re-read the clue once more, extra carefully, to be sure you picked the real meaning and not the flipped one
Why Step 3 matters

Opposite-pairs are a favourite trick in hard vocabulary questions because BOTH words are usually ones you're less familiar with — so it's easy to be almost-sure without being fully sure. Spotting that an opposite pair even EXISTS among the options is a signal to slow down on that one question specifically, not to doubt your first read of the clue.

Spotting opposite pairs 🔎

Here are a few examples of the exact trap from the real competition paper — two words that mean nearly opposite things, both showing up as options for the same question:

WordIts near-opposite
paramount — most importantnegligible — so small it barely matters
elated — extremely happydespondent — extremely sad, without hope
ample — more than enoughscarce — not enough, hard to find
If two options in the same question look like they COULD mean opposite things, that is exactly the moment to slow down and re-quote the clue — not the moment to guess by "gut feeling."

Quick check — read the clue carefully, then pick the meaning it actually supports:

1. "After the coach explained the new plan clearly, the team's mood turned despondent when they realised how little time was left to prepare." What does "despondent" mean here?
2. "Even after the storm, the reservoir held an ample supply of water for the whole village." What does "ample" mean here?
3. "Getting the antibiotics to the clinic on time was paramount — every other task that day could wait." What does "paramount" mean here?

Full practice — fresh words, real options 📖

Priya's older cousin was widely known to be resolute — once she had decided to finish a project, nothing could talk her out of quitting early. Her younger brother, on the other hand, often gave up after one attempt, and their grandmother gently teased that his effort at chores was usually futile, since he abandoned each task before it was ever close to done. At the family meeting, their uncle was refreshingly candid about the business's problems, telling everyone the exact numbers instead of softening the truth. The old photographs he brought, though decades old, were still vivid, the colours as bright as the day they were taken. Compared to previous years, this year's harvest from the family farm was disappointingly meager, filling barely half of the usual storage barn. Despite the risk, their cousin's decision to sail across the bay alone was seen by everyone as wildly audacious.
