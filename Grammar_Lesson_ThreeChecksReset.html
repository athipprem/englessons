Check #1 — Finding the ONE error among four sentences 🔍

This question type shows you 4 different if-sentences that all LOOK similar, and asks you to find the single one with a mistake. The trap is trying to spot it "by feel" — the fix is a slow, mechanical routine you run on EVERY option, every time, no skipping.

1Look at the RESULT half of THIS option. Does it say "would have + done-word" (Third-shape result) or plain "would + word" (Second-shape result)?
2Look at the IF half of THIS option. Does it say "had + done-word" (Third-shape if-half) or plain past tense like "extended"/"arrived" (Second-shape if-half)?
3Do the two halves match? Third result needs a Third if-half. Second result needs a Second if-half — UNLESS there's a present-time word like "today" or "now," which allows the Mixed exception (Third if-half + Second result).
4If they DON'T match (and there's no present-time word) — that option is the error. Move to the next option and repeat Steps 1–3. Don't stop after finding one that "looks fine" — check ALL FOUR before deciding.
🔑 The mistake this fixes

It's tempting to scan quickly and pick whichever option "feels off." That works sometimes, but not reliably — the fix is running Steps 1–3 on literally every option, out loud in your head, before deciding. Slower but far more accurate.

Watch the routine in action 👀

Option A: "If the pipes had frozen, the plumber would have been called immediately." Result = "would have been called" (Third-shape). If-half = "had frozen" (Third-shape). MATCH — clean.

Option B: "If the alarm had sounded, the guards would check every exit." Result = "would check" (plain would, Second-shape). If-half = "had sounded" (Third-shape). Any present-time word? No. MISMATCH — this is the error.

Option C: "If the roof had leaked, the ceiling would have been damaged." Result = "would have been damaged" (Third-shape). If-half = "had leaked" (Third-shape). MATCH — clean.

Option D: "If the crew had arrived on time, the show would have started as planned." Result = "would have started" (Third-shape). If-half = "had arrived" (Third-shape). MATCH — clean.

Answer: B. The key: even after finding A clean, we still fully checked C and D before locking in B — never stop early.

Check #3 — "-ing" vs "to" after regret, for good this time 📸

This one keeps slipping, so let's build a picture that's hard to forget. Meet two versions of the same person: Photo-Album Rosa and Announcement Rosa.

📷 Photo-Album Rosa → "-ING"
Rosa is flipping through an old photo ALBUM, lookING back at something she already did, feeling sorry about it.

"Rosa regretted selling her old bike." (she already sold it — she's looking back)
📢 Announcement Rosa → "TO"
Rosa is reading a note TO an audience, right now, announcing bad news formally as it happens.

"We regret to inform you the shop is closing." (announcing it fresh, right now)
🔑 The one question

Ask: "has this already happened, and are they looking back on it?" → Photo-Album Rosa → -ING. Ask: "are they announcing something unwelcome right now, formally?" → Announcement Rosa → TO. Almost every sentence you'll see is Photo-Album Rosa — the TO version is rare and usually sounds like a formal apology.
