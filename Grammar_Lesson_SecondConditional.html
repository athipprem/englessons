Second Conditional

Imagining situations that are not real — right now or in the future.
🚀 New Grammar — builds directly on Unit 2 Zero & First Conditionals
Section 1

💡 What Is the Second Conditional?

You already know two conditionals. The Zero Conditional describes things that are always true. The First Conditional describes things that are real and possible in the future. The Second Conditional is different: it describes situations that are imaginary, unreal, or hypothetical — things that are not actually happening and probably won't happen.

The Key Idea

Second Conditional = "What if...?" It talks about an imaginary situation and its imaginary result. You are not describing reality — you are imagining a different one.

Examples of Second Conditional thinking:

"If I were taller, I would join the basketball team." (I am NOT tall enough — this is imaginary)

"If she lived in Paris, she would visit the Eiffel Tower every week." (She does NOT live in Paris — imaginary)

"If we had a bigger garden, we would grow vegetables." (We do NOT have a bigger garden — imaginary)

Section 2

✎ The Formula

If-clause
If + past simple
,
Result clause
would + base verb

The if-clause uses past simple — but it does NOT mean past time! It is a special use of the past form to signal "this is imaginary."

The result clause always uses would + base verb.

The Most Common Mistake

NEVER put "would" in the if-clause. "If I would live there..." is always wrong. The if-clause uses past simple only: "If I lived there..."

The "were" rule — special case for "to be"

When the verb in the if-clause is "to be," use were for all persons — not "was":

✓ "If I were you, I would apologise."    ✓ "If she were here, she would know what to do."

✗ "If I was you..."    ✗ "If she was here..." (technically accepted in informal speech, but always use were in written English)

Quick Memory Rule

If-clause: past simple (NOT would). Result clause: would + base verb (NOT will). "If I had a million dollars, I would travel the world." → past simple in if-clause, would + travel in result.

Section 3

⇆ First Conditional vs Second Conditional

This is the key distinction you need for the test. Both talk about future situations — but one is real and one is imaginary.

First Conditional Second Conditional
Meaning Real possibility — could actually happen Imaginary / unreal — not actually happening
If-clause If + present simple If + past simple
Result clause will + base verb would + base verb
Example "If it rains, I will stay inside."
(Rain is possible — real)		 "If it rained every day, I would move to Spain."
(Not actually raining every day — imaginary)
Clue words tomorrow, soon, this week, next year I wish, imagine if, what if, if only
The Quick Test

Ask: "Is the situation in the if-clause actually possible right now or in the future?" If YES → First Conditional. If NO (it's imaginary or unlikely) → Second Conditional.

Section 4

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1 — First or Second?
"If the team wins tomorrow, they will enter the national finals." vs "If the team won every match, they would become legends."
1
First sentence: "wins" = present simple; "will enter" = will + base verb. Formula: First Conditional.
2
Is it real? Yes — tomorrow's match is genuinely possible. ✓ First Conditional = real future possibility.
3
Second sentence: "won" = past simple; "would become" = would + base verb. Formula: Second Conditional.
4
Is it real? No — winning every match is imaginary/hypothetical. ✓ Second Conditional = unreal situation.
✓ First: wins/will enter (real). Second: won/would become (imaginary).
Example 2 — Fill in the Blank
"If she _______ (be) the school principal, she would change the lunch menu."
1
The result clause uses "would change" → this is Second Conditional.
2
Second Conditional if-clause needs past simple. Verb = "to be".
3
Special rule: for "to be" in Second Conditional, use "were" for all persons.
4
Answer: "If she were the school principal..."
✓ Answer: "were" — use "were" (not "was") for all persons with "to be" in Second Conditional.

Summary

✅ Key Takeaways

The Formula

  • If + past simple, would + base verb
  • NEVER "would" in the if-clause
  • NEVER "will" in the if-clause
  • Use "were" (not "was") for all persons with "to be"

When to Use It

  • Imaginary situations not currently true
  • Unlikely or hypothetical futures
  • "What if...?" thinking
  • Clue: the if-clause describes something not real

First vs Second

  • First: real/possible → present simple + will
  • Second: imaginary/unreal → past simple + would
  • Test: "Could this actually happen?" Yes = First. No = Second.

Common Errors

  • "If I would..." → always wrong
  • "If she was..." → use "were" in formal/written English
  • "If he adopted... he will rise" → must be "would rise"
  • Check both clauses before finalising
