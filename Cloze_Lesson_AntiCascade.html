Why this matters 🌍

Imagine you're doing a big puzzle with a friend, and one piece just won't fit no matter what you try. If you get frustrated and start yanking OTHER pieces out that were already fine, you can wreck the whole puzzle over one tricky piece. The same thing can happen in a cloze passage: one hard gap makes you doubt yourself, and suddenly gaps you totally knew start feeling shaky too. Today's lesson gives you a tool to stop that from happening — in cloze passages, in real books, and honestly in a lot of life: one hard moment doesn't have to wreck the ones after it.

What actually happened last time 🔎

On your last big test, one gap needed a word you'd only ever seen as a trap before. Getting that one unfamiliar gap wrong seems to have shaken your confidence for a few gaps right after it — the answers you gave next were still real, sensible connector words, just not the ones each gap actually needed. That's not "forgetting the method" — it's the puzzle-piece problem from the start of this lesson. Step 3 above (box it, don't erase) is your main defence. Today we also remove the ONE unfamiliar trap that started it.

New trick: "despite" can be the RIGHT answer too 🔑

Every single time you've seen "despite" before, it was the distractor — the one word in the bank that never fits. That made total sense, because in every passage so far, every gap needed to join TWO FULL IDEAS (a subject + a verb on each side). "Despite" can't do that job. But "despite" is a real, useful word — it just does a DIFFERENT job than although/however. Today you learn what that job is, so "despite" stops being a total mystery and becomes just another tool.

ALTHOUGH / THOUGH — needs a full ideaDESPITE / IN SPITE OF — needs a THING, not a full idea
Although it rained all day, the match went ahead.
(it rained all day = a full idea: subject "it" + verb "rained")		Despite the rain, the match went ahead.
(the rain = just a THING, no verb doing an action)
Although she was exhausted, Maya finished the race.
(she was exhausted = a full idea)		Despite being exhausted, Maya finished the race.
(being exhausted = an -ING word, still just a THING, no separate subject)
The 3-second check: look at what comes right AFTER the gap.
Is it a full sentence-shape (a subject doing a verb)? → although/however.
Is it just a thing, a name, or an -ING word (no subject+verb of its own)? → despite.

Quick check — full idea, or just a thing/-ing word?

1. "___ the heavy traffic, the bus arrived on time." — what follows the gap?
2. "___ the alarm rang twice, nobody in the office noticed it." — what follows the gap?
3. "___ losing the first game, the team went on to win the tournament." — what follows the gap?

Now pick the actual word — despite as the hero, not just the villain 🦸

Same test as before (what follows the gap?) — but now choose between despite and its full-idea twin, although. Remember: "despite" is a preposition here, not a clause-joiner, so it never counts toward the four families (CONTRAST/RESULT/TIME/ADDITION) in the same way — think of it as a 5th, special CONTRAST tool just for THINGS and -ING words.

1. "___ the short notice, most of the class still turned up for the extra rehearsal."
2. "___ the notice was short, most of the class still turned up for the extra rehearsal."
3. "___ having very little practice, the choir sounded confident on stage."
4. "___ they had very little practice, the choir sounded confident on stage."

The real test — anti-cascade practice passage 🧩

🧩 Gap 3 is the tricky one — on purpose
Gap 3 needs "despite," the word you're least used to seeing as a correct answer. Do your best on it, then BOX your answer and move on. Gaps 4, 5, 6, and 7 are separate questions — answer each one completely fresh, the same way you would if gap 3 didn't exist.

How did the "hard gap" affect the rest? 📊

"Despite" + a thing or an -ing word = correct. "Although/however" + a full idea = correct. And whatever happens on ONE gap, box your answer and give the very next gap a completely fresh look — that's the whole anti-cascade habit.
