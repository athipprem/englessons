Why this matters 🌍

Think about two kettles. One kettle "had boiled" — it did that all by itself, nobody helped it. A cup "had been filled" — someone had to actually pour the water in; the cup couldn't fill itself. Both sentences describe something that already finished happening, but they're built slightly differently because of WHO or WHAT did the action. Grammar sentences work the same way — and today's lesson stops one word ("been") from tricking you into thinking a perfectly correct sentence is broken.

🚫 MYTH-BUSTER: "been" is NOT one of the checks

Some verbs describe something that DOES the action itself — call these DOER verbs. Other verbs describe something that has the action done TO it by someone or something else — call these GETTER verbs. GETTER verbs need "been" as a normal, built-in part of their grammar. DOER verbs don't. Neither one is more "correct" than the other — they're just different jobs.

DOER — no "been" neededGETTER — needs "been"
the booth had openedthe door had been opened (by staff)
the tickets had sold outthe tickets had been printed (by a machine)
the choir had rehearsedthe set had been painted (by the crew)
the queue had formedthe hall had been decorated (by volunteers)

The quick test: try adding "...by someone" after the verb. If it makes sense, it's a GETTER (needs "been"). If it sounds silly, it's a DOER (no "been" needed) — "the booth had opened by someone" sounds odd (the booth just opens), but "the door had been opened by someone" makes perfect sense.

Watch it worked out — a DOER hiding among GETTERs 👀

"Which sentence contains a grammar error?" A. If the raffle tickets had sold out, the organisers would have printed more. B. If the backstage door had been left open, cold air would blow through the hall. C. If the hall had better heating, the guests would feel warmer during the interval. D. If the microphone stand had been fixed, the sound would have carried better.

First glance: A says "had sold out" — no "been" — while B and D both say "had been ___". It's tempting to circle A right away because it looks different from its neighbours. Stop — "been" isn't a check.

Check A properly: "had sold out" is a DOER verb (tickets sell out by themselves — "had sold out by someone" sounds silly). "will"? No. Broken verb? No. Halves match? "had sold out" (Third if-half) + "would have printed" (Third result) → matching Third. Passes all three — A is clean.

Now check the rest: C = "had better heating" (past simple) + "would feel" (would+verb) → matching Second, clean. D = "had been fixed" (Third) + "would have carried" (Third) → matching, clean. B = "had been left open" (GETTER, Third if-half) + "would blow" (plain would, Second result) → halves DON'T match. B fails check 3 — that's the real error.

The lesson: A "looked" suspicious for having no "been" — but it was a completely clean DOER sentence. B "looked" normal (it has "been" like everyone expects) — but it was the one that actually broke a real check. Only the three checks decide, never the presence of "been."

One more thing — the TODAY trap 📅

Sometimes a sentence mixes a past CAUSE with a present-day RESULT. The clue is a present-time word in the result half, like today, now, or these days. When you see one of those words, the result half should use plain would (not "would have"), even though the if-half is a normal Third ("had...").

📅 MIXED: past cause, present result
If + had (been) + past participle, … would + verb (+ today / now)

Example: "If the fire alarm had not gone off during rehearsal, the whole show would not be delayed today."

The if-half is a normal Third ("had not gone off" — something that already happened, or didn't). But the result word "today" tells you the delay is happening RIGHT NOW, in the present — so the result needs plain "would not be", NOT "would not have been." If you see "today/now" and still pick "would have been ___", the halves don't actually match the TIME the sentence is describing, even though they'd match a normal Third pattern.
