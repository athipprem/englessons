Unit 17 · Lesson 2
Why this matters 🌍

You already know your conditionals really well — you can spot the type, catch a stray "will", and handle mixed sentences. The only thing that tripped you last time wasn't the grammar; it was the question. Some questions ask "which one is wrong?" and some ask "which one is right?" — and it's easy to hunt for the wrong target. One tiny habit fixes it completely.

Two opposite hunts 🔍

"Which contains an ERROR?" means find the one broken sentence. "Which is CORRECT?" means find the one clean sentence. Same grammar, opposite target. Today: one habit so you never mix them up. Next →

✏️ THE HABIT: before you look at the choices, circle the question word.
"ERROR" → hunt the broken one 🚫  ·  "CORRECT" → hunt the clean one ✅

🚫 "contains an ERROR?"

Three are fine, ONE is broken. Cross out the good ones; the broken one left is your answer.

✅ "is CORRECT?"

Three are broken, ONE is fine. Cross out the broken ones; the clean one left is your answer.

Drill 1 — which HUNT is it? 🎯

Just read the question and decide the target. (This is the habit — do it before every grammar question.)

1. "Which sentence contains a grammar error?"
2. "Which sentence is grammatically correct?"
3. "Which sentence has a mistake in it?"
4. "Which of these is written correctly?"

Drill 2 — the real thing 🧩

Circle the question word first, then cross out and choose. Notice the target flips on the last one.

✅ NOW THE OPPOSITE — which is CORRECT?
Circle the question word FIRST — ERROR or CORRECT. Then run the three checks and cross out. The grammar was never the problem; the target was.
