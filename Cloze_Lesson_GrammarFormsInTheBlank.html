New pattern #1 — the hyphen rule 🔗

Look at these two sentences: "She carried a 25-ounce bag." and "The bag weighed 25 ounces." Both are about the exact same bag, but one says "25-ounce" (hyphenated, singular, no -s) and the other says "25 ounces" (no hyphen, plural, with -s). Neither is a typo — they follow a real rule.

🔗 Number + Unit AS A DESCRIBER
When the number+unit sits directly in front of a noun, describing it like an adjective, HYPHENATE it and keep the unit SINGULAR (no -s) — even though the number is bigger than one.

a 25-ounce bag · a 10-year-old boy · a 5-mile hike
📏 Number + Unit AS A MEASUREMENT
When the number+unit stands on its own (usually after "is/was/weighed"), with NO noun right after it, there's no hyphen and the unit stays PLURAL as normal.

The bag weighed 25 ounces · He is 10 years old · They hiked 5 miles
Is there a NOUN right after the number+unit?
YES — describing that noun
Hyphenate + singular unit
NO — standing alone
No hyphen + plural unit

Practice — hyphen or no hyphen? 🎯

1. "The zoo just welcomed a ___ elephant calf." (2/week/old)
2. "By the time she finished the race, she had run ___." (10/mile)
3. "They built a sturdy ___ fence around the garden." (6/foot)
4. "The delivery driver said the parcel weighed almost ___." (3/kilogram)

New pattern #2 — the "recommend that" rule 📢

Read this sentence out loud: "The doctor recommended that he ___ more water." Your ear might want "drinks" (matching "he"). But the correct answer is actually "drink" — the plain, base form, with no -s, no matter who the subject is. This special pattern is called the subjunctive, and it only shows up after a small group of trigger words.

After recommend that / suggest that / insist that / require that / demand that — the verb goes back to its BASE FORM. No -s. No "to." No tense change. Every time, no matter the subject.
🔑 Why it feels wrong but is right

These trigger words are all about someone WANTING something to happen — not something that's already true. English marks that "wanted, not yet real" feeling by stripping the verb down to its bare form. Compare: "He drinks water" (a fact) vs. "I recommend that he drink water" (a wish/suggestion, base form).

Does the sentence use recommend / suggest / insist / require / demand + "that"?
YES
Use the BASE verb form (no -s, no "to")
NO
Use the normal tense as usual

Practice — spot the base form 🎯

2. "The notice recommends that visitors ___ (not/feed) the animals at the sanctuary." Which form?
3. "The airline requires that each passenger ___ (show) a valid ticket before boarding." Which form?
4. "My grandmother always suggests that we ___ (try) the soup before adding more salt." Which form?
