New idea #1: "-ing" or "to + verb" — it can change the WHOLE meaning 🔀

First, two brand-new words, explained simply. Look at these two words that can both follow a verb:

FormWhat it looks like
Gerunda verb with "-ing" added, acting like a thing: making, going, swimming, deciding
Infinitive"to" + the plain verb: to make, to go, to swim, to decide

Most verbs only allow ONE of these after them ("I enjoy swimming" — never "I enjoy to swim"). But a few special verbs allow BOTH — and here's the surprise: using the wrong one can completely change what the sentence means. The word "regret" is the clearest example:

regret + -ING = sorry about something ALREADY DONEregret + TO + verb = used ONLY for announcing bad news right now
"I regret leaving my umbrella at home." (I already left it — now I'm sorry about that past action.)"I regret to inform you that the trip is cancelled." (I am about to tell you something unwelcome — a special, fixed way of breaking bad news.)
"She regretted not studying harder for the test." (the not-studying already happened)"We regret to say that the show has been postponed." (announcing news right now)
🔑 The 3-second test

Ask: is the sentence looking BACK at something that already happened, and feeling sorry about it? → use -ING (regret making, regret losing, regret saying). Is the sentence a special, formal way of announcing unwelcome news RIGHT NOW? → use TO + verb (regret to inform, regret to say, regret to announce). The "announcing news" use is much rarer — if in doubt, and the sentence is just about something in the past, -ING is almost always correct.

Quick check — is this "looking back, sorry about the past" or "announcing news right now"?

1. "He regretted ___ (say) something so unkind to his sister the night before."
2. "We regret ___ (inform) passengers that the 6pm ferry has been cancelled."
3. "Looking back at his old photos, he regretted ___ (throw) away his childhood drawings."

Watch it worked out — spotting a wrong "to + verb" 👀

Sentence: "After years of hard work finally paying off, she deeply regretted to make the difficult decision to leave her hometown behind."

Step 1 — is this looking back, or announcing news? The decision has already been made ("after years... finally paying off"), and she is now feeling sorry about that PAST choice. That's looking-back, not an announcement.

Step 2 — pick the form: looking-back → needs the "-ing" form.

Step 3 — fix it: the correct form is "she deeply regretted making the difficult decision..."

The lesson: "regret + to" is real English — but only for formally announcing unwelcome news happening right now ("I regret to inform you..."). Everywhere else, if someone is looking back and feeling sorry about something already done, it's "regret + -ing".

New idea #2: some words secretly need an "s" 🔢

You already know most plurals are obvious — one cat, two cats. But some phrases involve MORE THAN ONE of something even though the sentence doesn't say a number out loud. Look at this:

SentenceWhat's really happening
"She paid off her new laptop in monthly installment."❌ Wrong — "monthly" already tells you this happens more than once (every month). If it repeats, there must be more than one instalment.
"She paid off her new laptop in monthly installments."✅ Correct — the word "monthly" is the clue: something that repeats every month is, by definition, more than one payment.
The 3-second test: look for a "repeating" or "counting" signal word right before the noun — words like monthly, weekly, several, various, multiple, a series of. If one of these appears, the noun almost always needs the plural "s", even if the sentence never says an exact number.
Signal wordExample
monthly / weekly / yearlypaid in monthly installments, held weekly meetings
several / various / multipletried several approaches, considered various options
a series ofa series of experiments, a series of delays

Quick check — does the signal word mean this happens more than once?

1. "The scientists ran several ___ (test) before publishing their results." Singular or plural?
2. "The town hall holds a weekly ___ (meeting) for local residents." Singular or plural?
3. "The bridge was closed after a series of ___ (inspection) revealed cracks in the concrete." Singular or plural?
