Subtle Vocabulary Inference

When the passage doesn't give you a direct clue — three advanced strategies for working out a word's meaning from hidden signals.
🎯 ISA Priority — Vocabulary
Section 1 of 5

📚 Why Some Words Are Harder to Infer

In Unit 4 you learned four strategies: Definition clue (a dash or "means"), Example clue (such as / including), Contrast clue (but / however / unlike), and General sense (what is happening in the story?).

Those four strategies work well when the passage is generous with information. But sometimes — especially in harder ISA passages — the writer gives you no definition, no example, and no contrast word. The clues are buried inside the passage itself.

The New Challenge

In difficult vocabulary questions, you will not see a dash (—), "means", "but", or any obvious signal. You have to read the surrounding text more carefully and use three hidden clue types to work out the meaning.

Section 2 of 5

🎯 Three Advanced Inference Strategies

Strategy 5

🎫 Tone / Mood Clue

What is the overall feeling of the passage — positive, negative, or mixed? The target word must match that tone. If the passage is describing something terrible, words with positive meanings are probably wrong.

"The flood [ravaged] the valley." → The tone is destructive and negative → ravaged = severely damaged. ✓
Strategy 6

👤 Behaviour / Action Clue

What does the character do? The target word describes the character — so it must match their specific actions, not just general personality. Look at what they actually do in the text.

"She checked every number three times before moving on." → She checks everything carefully → [meticulous] = very thorough and careful. ✓
Strategy 7

📈 Degree / Intensity Clue

Sometimes two answer options mean roughly the same thing, but one is stronger than the other. Use the passage to judge the intensity — extreme language in the text signals a stronger word.

"Despite three failed attempts, she continued without hesitation." → Three failures + no hesitation = very strong determination → [relentless] (not just "determined"). ✓
Strategy 8

🔢 Logical Elimination

When unsure, rule out options that contradict the text. If the passage says a character felt isolated, any answer option meaning "popular" or "sociable" can be eliminated immediately.

"No one visited. Letters went unanswered." → NOT friendly or sociable → eliminate those options → [reclusive] = avoiding contact with others. ✓

🔎 Reading the Hidden Clues in a Passage

Here is a short passage with three underlined words. The highlighted text around each word shows the type of clue being used.

Tone / Mood clue
Behaviour / Action clue
Degree / Intensity clue
Professor Ayasha was known for her scrupulous approach to research. She never published a finding without running it through at least four rounds of checking, and she had once delayed a major paper by six months to verify a single statistic. Her colleagues sometimes found her methods slow, but no error had ever appeared in her published work.

The data she uncovered was deeply unsettling. It suggested that hundreds of patients had been given the wrong diagnosis for over a decade. The implications were enormous and disturbing.

Despite working eighteen-hour days for three months, facing repeated setbacks, and receiving little institutional support, Professor Ayasha remained resolute in her determination to make the findings public.
How to read the clues

scrupulous: Behaviour clue — she runs four rounds of checking, delayed a paper six months over one statistic. Action = extreme care and thoroughness. → scrupulous = very careful and thorough.
unsettling: Tone clue — the passage describes wrong diagnoses, enormous implications, disturbing. The mood is alarming and negative. → unsettling = causing worry or alarm.
resolute: Degree/Intensity clue — eighteen-hour days for three months, repeated setbacks, little support — yet she continued. This is extreme persistence against extreme obstacles. → resolute = firmly determined, unwilling to give up.

📝 Worked Example — Full Process

Worked Example — using Behaviour Clue + Elimination

Read this extract. What does the underlined word most likely mean?

"The general was [formidable]. His troops had never lost a battle under his command. Enemy commanders would withdraw rather than face him, and his very name was enough to discourage an attack."

Options: A. Kind and popular   B. Inspiring respect and fear   C. Reckless and unpredictable   D. Cautious and defensive

1
Behaviour clue: What does he DO? Never lost a battle. Enemies withdraw rather than face him. His name discourages attacks.
2
Tone check: The passage describes him as powerful and intimidating — enemies are afraid of him.
3
Elimination: A (kind/popular) — eliminated: the passage says nothing about kindness. C (reckless) — eliminated: winning every battle is not reckless. D (cautious/defensive) — eliminated: his name discourages attacks, he is not hiding.
4
→ B (inspiring respect and fear) matches all clues: undefeated + feared by enemies.
✓ Answer: B. Inspiring respect and fear — formidable = something that causes admiration or fear because of impressive power or skill.

Three New Strategies

  • Tone/Mood clue — does the passage feel positive, negative, or alarming?
  • Behaviour/Action clue — what does the character actually DO?
  • Degree/Intensity clue — how extreme is the language around the word?

Logical Elimination

  • Cross out options that contradict the text
  • Cross out options that are too mild or too strong
  • The remaining option is your answer

The Full Inference Toolkit (Units 4 + 5)

  • Definition clue: dash (—) or "means" / "in other words" gives the meaning directly
  • Example clue: "such as" or "including" — identify what the examples have in common
  • Contrast clue: "but", "however", "unlike", "although" — the word means the opposite
  • General sense: what is happening in the sentence overall?
  • Tone/Mood: match the word to the feeling of the passage
  • Behaviour/Action: match the word to what the character does
  • Degree/Intensity: use the extremity of the language as a guide
