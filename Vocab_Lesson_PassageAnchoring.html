Passage Anchoring

The answer is always in the passage. Find the exact clue, quote it to yourself, then match it to the option. Never guess from the general feeling of the text.
⚓ Quote and Match — Every Time
Section 1 of 5

🔎 Why Guessing Goes Wrong

Vocabulary inference questions look like meaning questions but they are actually clue-finding questions. The answer is never just what the word means in general — it is what the passage specifically tells you about the character or situation at that moment.

In Unit 7, Q16 had a near-definition in the passage: "not frightened exactly, but stripped of all thought. He could not even remember how his presentation began." The correct answer (D) echoed this almost word-for-word: "stripped of thought, unable to react normally." Prem chose C (surprised and unprepared) — a guess from context rather than a specific clue found in the text.

The Guessing Trap

When you read the passage and choose an answer based on the general feeling of the situation, you are guessing. The trap options are designed to match the general situation. Only the correct answer matches the specific clue in the text.

The Core Principle

Every correct answer for a vocabulary question can be justified by quoting specific words from the passage. If you cannot find a quote that directly supports your choice, you have not found the anchor yet.

Section 2 of 5

⚓ The Quote and Match Method — 3 Steps

Use this method for every vocabulary question. Do not skip any step.

1
Find the target word in the passage
Go back to the exact line where the underlined word appears. Read the full sentence — not just the word itself — and the sentence before and after it.
2
Find and quote the specific clue
Look for: a near-definition (the passage restates the word in simpler language), body language or behaviour clues, a direct statement of how the character feels, or a contrast clue (the passage tells you what the character is NOT). Quote it to yourself: "The clue is __________."
3
Match the clue to the option — not to the situation
Read each option and ask: "Does this option match my quoted clue?" The correct option reflects the specific clue, not just the general situation. Eliminate options that describe the situation but contradict the clue.
Section 3 of 5

💡 Four Types of Passage Clues

Every vocabulary question has at least one of these clue types. Learn to spot each one:

📚 Near-Definition
The passage restates the target word in simpler words, often right next to it or in the next clause.
Example: "numb — not frightened, but stripped of all thought." The passage defines numb for you.
🧠 Body Language / Behaviour Clue
The character's physical actions, expressions, or movements tell you their inner state.
Example: "pacing, checking watch every few seconds, unable to sit still" = restless/anxious inner state.
❓ Contrast Clue
The passage tells you what the character is NOT, which helps eliminate wrong options.
Example: "not frightened exactly" — eliminates frightened as a synonym; points to something quieter.
🌡️ Degree / Intensity Clue
How extreme or strong is the language? Extreme language points to stronger emotion words.
Example: "could not even remember how her presentation began" = total mental blankness, not just surprise.
🌟 Worked Example — Quote and Match in Action
Kai felt immediately [numb] — not frightened exactly, but stripped of all thought. He could not even remember how his presentation began.
Step 1 — Find the word
Target word: numb. Found on line 1. Read the full sentence and surrounding context.
Step 2 — Quote the clue
Near-definition clue: "stripped of all thought. He could not even remember how his presentation began." — This is a near-definition of numb.
Step 3 — Match to options
A. calm and still — no; the passage shows loss of thought, not calm. B. frightened and confused — no; the passage says "not frightened exactly." C. surprised and unprepared — no; there is no mention of surprise. D. stripped of thought, unable to react normally — YES, this echoes the passage almost word-for-word.
⚓ Anchor: "stripped of all thought / could not even remember" → matches D exactly. Answer: D.
Section 4 of 5

🧠 Inner State vs Quality vs Action — The Ongoing Trap

The near-synonym trap for vocabulary words often comes in three forms. Knowing which type the target word is tells you which options to eliminate immediately.

TypeWhat it describesExamplesClue to look for
Inner State How the character feels right now — their emotional experience numb, apprehensive, pensive, composed, dazed, warmth Body language, facial expression, direct statement of feeling
Quality / Trait A characteristic or ability the person has (more permanent) focused, determined, capable, skilled, methodical What the person is like in general, not how they feel in this moment
Action / Behaviour What the character does — their observable actions prepared (what they did), practised, worked hard What the person does — their behaviour described in the passage
Elimination Shortcut

If the target word is an inner state word, immediately eliminate any option that describes an action or a quality. The correct option must name a feeling or emotional experience — not what the person does or what kind of person they are.

The Most Common Error Pattern

Choosing the option that describes what the character DID (their preparation, their action) instead of how they FELT (their inner state) in that moment. For example, choosing "confident and well-prepared" for apprehensive — that describes the preparation (action), not the feeling of nervousness that the word actually names.

Quote and Match — 3 Steps

  • Step 1: Find the word in the passage — read full sentence + surrounding
  • Step 2: Quote the specific clue (near-def / body language / contrast)
  • Step 3: Match the clue to the option — not the general situation

State vs Action vs Quality

  • Inner state = how the character feels right now
  • Quality = a trait or characteristic (more permanent)
  • Action = what the character does or did
  • State words need feeling clues — not action or preparation clues
