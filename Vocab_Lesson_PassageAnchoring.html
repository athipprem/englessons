When should you choose your answer in a vocabulary question?

Why impression-matching fails 🚫

Three vocabulary questions wrong in Unit 8. Not because the words were too hard — but because you read the situation and matched by feel, instead of finding the exact clue phrase.

❌ Q18 — "grounded" — without Quote and Match:

Marcus was getting compliments. You thought: "He must feel relieved and happy." → Wrong. −1 mark.

With Quote and Match:
The colon is a near-definition signal. Quote what follows it: "his breathing slowed, his shoulders dropped, the fidgeting stopped."
Test option B: "calm and stable, settled inside" — does it match "breathing slowed, shoulders dropped"? → YES → Correct. +1 mark.

Never match by situation or mood.
Find the exact clue. Quote it. Then test each option word against it.

Quote and Match — 3 Steps 🎯

Every vocabulary question has a clue hidden in the passage — a phrase that directly explains or contrasts the target word. Your job is to find it, quote it, and match it to the options.

Step 1 — Find the word in context
Read the sentence containing the target word. Then read one sentence before and one after. The clue is usually within two sentences of the word.
Step 2 — Quote the exact clue phrase
Write down the specific phrase that explains the word. Do not paraphrase — write the actual words from the passage. If you cannot quote a specific phrase, you have not found the clue yet.
Step 3 — Test each option word against your quote
For each option: does this word match what the quote describes? If an option contains one correct element and one unsupported element — eliminate it.
Only choose an option where every element is supported by your quote.
⚠ The Inner-State Trap
If the target word names a feeling or state, eliminate any option that describes an action or quality instead.
State = what the character feels inside. Quality = a skill or characteristic. Action = something they do.
Clue: "his hands moving expressively" → animated = lively and expressive (state/manner) ✓ — NOT "enthusiastic and loud" (loud has no passage support)

The 4 Clue Types 🎨

Every passage clue is one of four types. Recognising which type you are looking at helps you quote the right phrase.

🟠 Near-Definition
The passage directly defines the word — often after a colon, dash, or "as if".

Signal: colon ( : ) or em dash ( — )
Example: "He felt numb — not frightened exactly, but stripped of all thought."
Quote: "stripped of all thought"
🟣 Contrast Clue
The passage describes what the word is NOT, using "but", "yet", "unlike", "instead of".

Signal: but / yet / unlike / instead
Example: "Not confident, but tentative — checking before each step."
Quote: "not confident... checking before each step"
🟢 Body Language Clue
The passage shows physical actions or expressions that reveal the inner state.

Signal: body parts, movement, facial expression
Example: "animated — his hands moving expressively as he explained."
Quote: "hands moving expressively"
🔵 Degree Clue
The passage shows the intensity or extent of the feeling.

Signal: "so... that", "such... that", "too... to"
Example: "She felt elated — a happiness so strong it felt too big to hold."
Quote: "happiness so strong it felt too big to hold"
