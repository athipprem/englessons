Two traps, one fix

TrapWhat it does
Word-feelThe word sounds like another word. (gruelling → "gruesome", dank → "dark")
Story-feelAn option fits the story — but the quote next to the word doesn't prove it. (the brother was wrong earlier, so "surprised" feels right)
The fix for both:
underline the quote, then tick your answer against it.

The Prove-It move ✅

The right answer is always sitting in the words right next to the target — the quote. Your chosen meaning must be something the quote actually shows. If you can't point to it, it's a trap.

Worked example — "elated"
"…she was elated, leaping so high that her badge flew off."

Underline the quote: leaping so high her badge flew off.
Tick each option against it:
• "extremely happy" → leaping for joy ✅ the quote shows this
• "surprised her brother was wrong" → the quote says nothing about her brother ❌ story-feel
• "relieved the day was over" → nothing about relief in the quote ❌ story-feel

The one rule
Your quote must contain the meaning. "It happened earlier in the story" is not proof. "It sounds like another word" is not proof. Only the quote counts.
