Near-Synonym Precision

When two options both seem right — how to find the one that exactly matches the passage clue.
🎯 ISA Priority — Vocabulary
Section 1 of 5

🤔 The New Challenge

You have already mastered the four inference strategies — definition clue, example clue, contrast clue, and general sense. You got 5/6 on the last vocabulary test. That is strong.

The one error you made — choosing A (careful and diplomatic) for the word "candid" when the correct answer was B (frank and direct) — shows the new challenge you are facing. The passage said: "she always said exactly what she thought, without hesitation or sugar-coating." That is a near-perfect description of "frank and direct". But "careful and diplomatic" also sounds like a positive quality — and that is the trap.

The Near-Synonym Trap

A near-synonym is a word that means something similar but not the same. In harder vocabulary questions, two of the four options will often sound plausible. One of them exactly matches the passage clue. The other sounds nice but does not. Your job is to find the one that matches the clue, not the one that sounds best.

Section 2 of 5

🔎 The Exact Match Rule

Here is the rule that eliminates the near-synonym trap every time:

🔎 The Exact Match Rule

Do not ask: "Which option sounds like a good description of this word?" Ask instead: "Which option matches the specific clue the passage gives me?" The passage gives you exact evidence. Find it. Match it precisely.

Here is an example of the trap in action:

✗ Wrong Approach

Passage: "She always said exactly what she thought, without hesitation or sugar-coating."

Student thinks: "Candid sounds like being confident and positive in communication… so I will choose: careful and diplomatic."

✓ Exact Match Approach

Passage: "She always said exactly what she thought, without hesitation or sugar-coating."

Student asks: "Which option exactly matches 'says exactly what she thinks, no hesitation, no softening'?" → frank and direct. ✓

"Careful and diplomatic" sounds like a positive quality — but diplomatic actually means careful not to offend, which is the opposite of saying exactly what you think without sugar-coating. The passage clue eliminates it precisely.

Section 3 of 5

⚠️ Three Types of Near-Synonym Trap

📈 Trap 1 — Degree Traps (similar meaning, different strength)

Two words mean the same thing, but one is much stronger. The passage clue tells you which strength is needed.

Example: words meaning "persistent"
determined
persistent
relentless
← gentler                                                     stronger →

If the passage says "she continued despite three major setbacks, two injuries, and a complete loss of funding" — the extreme language signals the strongest word. If it says "she stayed focused and kept going" — that signals something more moderate like "determined".

🔄 Trap 2 — Direction Traps (words that seem similar but mean opposite things)

candid

Says exactly what you think. Does not soften or hide opinions.

↔
diplomatic

Careful not to offend. Chooses words thoughtfully to avoid conflict.

Both sound like positive communication skills — but they point in opposite directions. The passage clue tells you which one fits.

👤 Trap 3 — Specificity Traps (one option is too general)

Two options both roughly fit but one is far too broad. "Talented" could describe anyone with any skill. "Adept at reading her audience" is much more specific. The passage clue gives you a specific behaviour — match it precisely, not broadly.

Section 4 of 5

📝 Worked Example — Using Exact Match

Worked Example — Near-Synonym Trap
"The young chef was (1)[methodical] in his approach — he measured every ingredient to the nearest gram, followed each step in the same order every time, and never skipped a stage no matter how much time pressure he was under."

Options: A. Creative and experimental   B. Working in an orderly, step-by-step way   C. Very hardworking and dedicated   D. Precise and unwilling to make mistakes

1
Find the specific clue: "followed each step in the same order every time, never skipped a stage" → this is about order and sequence, not just accuracy or hard work.
2
Test each option: A = creative/experimental — opposite of following the same order. C = hardworking and dedicated — too general, says nothing about sequence. D = precise and unwilling to make mistakes — sounds right but "precise" is about accuracy, not about following a specific order of steps.
3
Exact match: B = "working in an orderly, step-by-step way" — this matches "followed each step in the same order every time" exactly. ✓
✓ Answer: B. D is the near-synonym trap — "precise" sounds right, but the clue is about ORDER and SEQUENCE, not accuracy. Always match the specific clue, not the general feel.

You can now identify and defeat the near-synonym trap. Match the clue, not the feeling.

Three Types of Trap

  • Degree trap — both words similar, but one is stronger. Match the degree of language in the passage.
  • Direction trap — words sound similar but mean opposites (candid vs diplomatic).
  • Specificity trap — one option too general. Match the specific behaviour, not the broad feel.

The Exact Match Rule

  • Find the specific clue in the passage — a behaviour, an action, a result
  • Ask: which option matches THIS clue exactly?
  • Do NOT ask: which option sounds like a good word?
  • When two options both seem right — go back to the clue and be more precise
