Section 1 of 5

📄 Quick Recap — All Four Conditional Types

You have learned all four conditional types across Units 1–5. Here they are in one table. The key column is If-Clause Form — that is the first thing you identify every time.

TypeIf-Clause FormResult Clause FormWhen to use it
FirstIf + present simple
If it rains…		will + base verb
…it will flood.		Real future possibility
SecondIf + past simple
If I were taller…		would + base verb
…I would play basketball.		Imaginary present
ThirdIf + past perfect
If she had studied…		would have + past participle
…she would have passed.		Imaginary past → past result
MixedIf + past perfect
If she had studied…		would + base verb
…she would be a doctor now.		Imaginary past → present result

Notice: Third and Mixed have the same if-clause form. The only thing that separates them is the result clause — and a time signal word.

Section 2 of 5

📅 The Decision Flowchart — Two Questions, In Order

Whenever you see a conditional sentence — whether you are filling in a gap, identifying the type, or spotting an error — always ask these two questions in this order.

📅 The Conditional Decision Flowchart
Start here: look at the IF-CLAUSE first
❓ Question 1
What form is the if-clause verb?
● Simple Past (was, didn't, worked)

If + simple past verb
"If I were…" / "If she had…" (NOT had + done — just one verb)

● Past Perfect (had + past participle)

If + had + past participle
"If she had studied…" / "If it had not rained…"

📌 SECOND CONDITIONAL

Simple past if-clause → imaginary present. Result = would + base verb

"If I were taller, I would play basketball."
❓ Question 2 — only for Past Perfect if-clauses
Does the result clause have a present time signal?
Look for: now / today / still / currently
YES → MIXED

Past perfect if-clause + present time signal → present result

would + base verb
NO → THIRD

Past perfect if-clause + no time signal → past result

would have + past participle
💡 Why If-Clause First?

The if-clause tells you which group you are in (Second OR Third/Mixed). Only after that do you look at the result. If you look at the result first, you can confuse yourself. Always: if-clause → then result.

Section 3 of 5

🚫 The "Will Is Banned" Rule

There is one more simple rule that eliminates wrong answer choices in seconds.

🚫 In Second, Third, and Mixed Conditionals — "will" NEVER appears in the result clause
would will have gone → This is never correct in Second, Third, or Mixed
will have been → would have been Replace "will" with "would"
will be → would be In conditional result clauses, only "would"

When you see a multiple-choice conditional question, scan the result clause of each option first. Any option with "will" in the result clause of a Second, Third, or Mixed conditional can be crossed out immediately.

The Only Exception

"Will" is correct in First Conditional results: "If it rains, we will cancel the match." First Conditional = real future possibility. Only in Second, Third, and Mixed does "would" replace "will".

Section 4 of 5

📝 Worked Examples — Flowchart in Action

Example 1 — What type is this?
"If the museum had kept the original paintings, visitors _______ them on display today."
1
If-clause: "had kept" → had + past participle → Past Perfect → Third or Mixed group.
2
Time signal in result? "today" → YES → MIXED CONDITIONAL.
3
Result form: Mixed → would + base verb. Base verb = "see". → "would still see"
✓ Answer: "…visitors would still see them on display today."
Example 2 — Spot the error
Which is wrong?  A: "If she had taken the offer, she would be living in Singapore now."  B: "If she had taken the offer, she will be living in Singapore now."
1
Both if-clauses: "had taken" → Past Perfect → Third or Mixed group.
2
Time signal: "now" → Mixed → result = would + base verb.
3
Sentence A: "would be living" ✓ — correct Mixed result.
Sentence B: "will be living" ✗ — "will" is banned in Mixed/Third result clauses.
✓ Sentence B is wrong. Replace "will" with "would" → "she would be living in Singapore now."
Example 3 — If-clause type error
"If the dam was not built in time, the valley would have flooded." Is the if-clause correct for a Third Conditional?
1
Result clause: "would have flooded" → Third Conditional result (no time signal, past result).
2
If-clause check: Third Conditional needs Past Perfect (had + past participle). "Was not" is simple past → Second Conditional if-clause. Wrong group for a Third Conditional result.
3
Correction: Replace "was not" with "had not been" → Past Perfect → matches the Third Conditional result.
✓ Corrected: "If the dam had not been built in time, the valley would have flooded." — if-clause must be Past Perfect for Third/Mixed Conditional.
