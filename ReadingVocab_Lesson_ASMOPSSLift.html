Why this matters 🌍

Grown-ups read dense, fact-heavy writing all the time — news articles, museum plaques, park notices, instructions. The skill isn't reading every word slowly; it's spotting the ONE big idea, understanding WHY the writer included certain details, and figuring out things the text doesn't say outright. This is also the exact skill that separated your ASMOPSS practice score (a strong 24/30) from a gold medal — reading was the one section that needs the biggest lift. Today we start building it, gently.

Four questions, four jobs 🌳

Read the question first
↓
What is it really asking?
"mainly about" / "best title"
MAIN IDEA — the one big point
"why did the writer include..."
AUTHOR'S PURPOSE — what job that detail does
"what can you conclude / infer"
INFERENCE — reason it out from clues, even though it's not stated directly
"according to the notice/sign"
REAL-WORLD TEXT — find the exact rule stated
Question typeWhat to do
Main ideaIgnore small details. Ask: "if I had to tell a friend this passage in ONE sentence, what would I say?" The right answer covers the WHOLE passage, not just one paragraph.
Author's purposeAsk: "why did the writer bother to put THIS detail in?" — often to show a contrast, prove a point, or set up a surprise.
InferenceThe answer isn't written word-for-word — combine two clues from the text yourself. Never pick an answer that fights the text, even if it "feels" plausible.
Real-world text (notices, notes)These are about precise reading — check who does what, when, and any exceptions ("except", "only", "unless"). Read it like a rule, not a story.

Watch it worked out 👀

Passage: "The bakery had stood empty for two years. Some said it should be knocked down. Instead, a group of neighbours repainted it themselves and reopened it as a free library."

Main idea: ignore the small detail (paint colour, exact time) — the whole passage is building toward: neighbours turned an empty building into a free library.

Author's purpose (why mention "some said it should be knocked down"?): to set up a contrast — showing the surprising, better outcome that actually happened, not the expected one.

Inference (what does this suggest about the neighbours?): the text never says "they cared about their community" directly — but repainting a building themselves and giving it away for free strongly implies exactly that. That's inference: combining clues, not quoting them.

Practice — From Quarry to Park 🏞️

For nearly a hundred years, the old stone quarry on the edge of town produced blocks for building. Trucks rumbled in and out every day, and the deep pit grew wider each year. But by the 1980s, the stone had run out, and the quarry closed. For over a decade afterwards, it sat empty — a scarred, grey hole surrounded by rusting fences.

In 1995, the town council proposed something nobody expected: instead of filling the pit or leaving it fenced off, they would turn it into a public park. Critics said it was a waste of money — nobody would want to visit a hole in the ground. But the council pointed out that the quarry already had steep natural walls, a flat floor perfect for playing fields, and a spring that could be turned into a lake. Rather than build a park from nothing, they would work with the shape the quarry had already carved.

Over the next five years, workers planted thousands of trees along the quarry walls, built winding paths down to the floor, and let the spring fill a shallow lake. Today, the old quarry is one of the town's most-loved green spaces. Visitors often say they would never guess the peaceful lake and shaded paths were once a noisy, dusty pit.

The park's transformation is now studied by other towns hoping to reuse their own abandoned quarries and mines, rather than simply burying them under rubble.

1. Main idea — What is this passage MAINLY about?
2. Author's purpose — Why does the writer most likely include what the critics said?
3. Inference — What can you infer about why the council chose to "work with the shape the quarry had already carved" rather than build a park from scratch?
