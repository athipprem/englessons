Why this matters 📖

You've already learned to spot a story's setting, plot, tone, and theme. Today we add one more skill on top: figuring out WHY a character does what they do — their motivation. This is one of the biggest jumps between "reading" and "reading closely," and it's exactly the kind of question that shows up in tougher comprehension passages. Once you can explain a character's motivation, you understand the story at a much deeper level.

What is "character motivation"? 🎯

Motivation is the REASON a character does something — what they want, or what feeling is pushing them to act. Authors rarely say the reason directly. You have to piece it together like a detective, using three clues: what the character DOES, what they SAY, and what happens AFTERWARD.

To find a character's motivation, ask:
↓
What does the character do or say that seems important?
↓
What do they seem to WANT — a feeling, a person's approval, to fix something, to prove something?
↓
Does the story's ending or outcome support that guess?
↓
That "want" is the motivation.

Two kinds of motivation 🧭

Motivations usually come from one of two places:

🌍 EXTERNAL
Comes from OUTSIDE the character — a rule, a person telling them to, a prize, a deadline.

"Coach said everyone had to run the extra lap, so Dan ran it."
💛 INTERNAL
Comes from INSIDE the character — a feeling like love, pride, guilt, fear, or wanting to prove something to themselves.

"Dan ran the extra lap even though no one was watching, because he wanted to prove to himself he could."
🔑 How to tell them apart

Ask: "if nobody else was around, would the character still do it?" If YES, it's likely INTERNAL (a feeling). If they'd only do it because someone told them to or something outside is forcing it, it's EXTERNAL.

Worked example 👀

"Every morning before school, Tara watered the row of sunflowers she had planted along the fence. Nobody had asked her to. Her little brother teased her about it, saying flowers were boring. But Tara kept going out, rain or shine, remembering how her father used to grow sunflowers in their old garden before they moved."

What does she do? Waters sunflowers every morning, even though nobody asked and her brother teases her.
What might she want? A connection to her father and their old garden — a memory, not approval from anyone else.
Does the outcome support it? Yes — she keeps going despite teasing, which only makes sense if the reason comes from inside her, not from wanting outside approval.
Answer: INTERNAL motivation — she's keeping a memory of her father alive.

"Malee practiced her violin for two hours every night, even after her fingers ached. Her parents never made her — in fact, they sometimes told her to rest. She kept thinking about the school concert, and how badly she wanted to play the solo perfectly, just for herself."

Is Malee's motivation external or internal?

Full story practice 📚

The Lantern Contest

Mali's grandmother had taught her how to fold paper lanterns since she was five years old, sitting together at the kitchen table every autumn evening. Delicate creases, careful folds, a candle glowing inside — Grandma always said a good lantern should look like it was made of light itself.

Then, in September, Grandma moved two streets away to live with Mali's aunt, in a part of town where she knew almost no one. On their weekend video calls, Grandma still smiled, but her voice had lost some of its usual sparkle. She mentioned the new neighbours politely, never warmly. Mali noticed, and it worried her.

When the community centre announced its annual Lantern Festival contest, Mali signed up immediately — not because a teacher assigned it, and not really for the small trophy either. She spent three weekends practicing, ruining a dozen sheets of paper before her folds finally matched the ones Grandma had shown her years ago.

On the night of the festival, Mali carried her finished lantern — glowing gold, folded exactly the way Grandma had taught her — straight to her grandmother's new street and set it on the porch railing, lit and glowing, facing the window. A moment later, the porch light switched on, and Grandma stepped outside, staring at the lantern with her hand pressed to her chest, smiling the widest smile Mali had seen in months.

1. (Setting) Where does most of the story's action lead Mali to travel?
2. (Motivation) Why does Mali really enter the lantern contest?
3. (Internal or external?) Is Mali's motivation internal or external?
4. (Tone) Which word best matches the tone of the story's final paragraph?
5. (Theme) Which message fits this story best?
6. (Vocabulary) Grandma's voice "had lost some of its usual sparkle." A word close in meaning to how Grandma felt is "wistful" (a quiet, gentle sadness, often missing something). Which word means roughly the OPPOSITE of wistful?

Great work! 🎉

To find motivation: notice what a character does and says, guess what they want, then check if the ending supports it. Ask "would they still do it if nobody was watching?" to tell internal from external.
