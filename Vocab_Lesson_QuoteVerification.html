Know Your Enemy — the Two Traps 🪤

Test writers (me included!) build wrong answers to catch students who skip the final check. There are exactly two kinds, and you met one of each in Unit 9.

🔊 The Word-Feel Trap
The wrong option matches what the word sounds like. "Eloquent" sounds posh → "formal and difficult." "Painstaking" sounds painful → "full of suffering." The trap works ONLY if you never look back at the quote.
📖 The Topic-Feel Trap
The wrong option matches the story, but not the quote. The character DID come a long way — but the clue sentence says nothing about surprise. Emotionally plausible, textually unsupported.
✅ The full method — Quote and Match, with the courtroom finish

Step 1 — Find: locate the sentence(s) that describe the word. Sometimes 1–2 sentences away.
Step 2 — Quote: read that sentence AND the one before it. That is your evidence.
Step 3 — Prove: for the option you like, ask: "Does my quote CONTAIN this meaning?" Not the story. Not the word's sound. The quote. If no — dismissed, next option.

The tell-tale sign you're being trapped
If you catch yourself thinking "well, he WAS proud / it WAS a competition / the word DOES sound fancy…" — stop. None of those are quotes. Evidence or it didn't happen.

Full Practice — The Night Market 🌙

Test conditions: clues sit 1–2 sentences from the word, and each question hides one trap. Read the passage once, then quote-and-prove every answer.

Tom's first shift at his aunt's night-market food stall did not begin well. He was (1) apprehensive — he kept wiping his hands on his apron, rereading the price list, and glancing at the queue that was already forming. His aunt, by contrast, worked the wok like a drummer. Her movements were (2) deft: she flipped the noodles, caught the bottle of sauce without looking, and plated each order in seconds. An hour in, a customer claimed loudly that he had been given the wrong change. The man's tone was (3) belligerent — he leaned over the counter, jabbed his finger at the till, and demanded the manager twice. Tom counted the coins again with shaking hands. The total was exactly right. His aunt's reply was (4) measured. She spoke slowly and evenly, thanked the man for checking, and laid the receipt flat on the counter for everyone to see. The man left without another word. By closing time, Tom had served forty customers on his own. His aunt watched him wipe down the counter and smiled. Years of burns, late nights and impossible customers had taught her which helpers would come back tomorrow. Tom, she decided, was (5) tenacious — the queue had rattled him, the angry man had shaken him, and he had not stopped serving once.
Q1. What does "apprehensive" most likely mean?
Q2. What does "deft" most likely mean? (word-feel trap inside)
Q3. What does "belligerent" most likely mean?
Q4. What does "measured" most likely mean? (word-feel trap inside)
Q5. What does "tenacious" most likely mean? (topic-feel trap inside)

Production Task ✍️ — your turn to write

Write ONE sentence using tenacious that SHOWS its meaning — a reader who has never seen the word should be able to guess it from your sentence alone.

Weak: "My friend is tenacious." (shows nothing) · Strong: "Even after failing the jump twelve times, the tenacious skater dusted herself off and tried a thirteenth."

Self-check — be honest, tick all three:
☐ Does my sentence show someone continuing after difficulty or failure?
