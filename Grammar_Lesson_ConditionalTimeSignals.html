Conditional Time Signals & the "had" Test

Two targeted fixes: spot the time signal to choose the right result form, and tell the difference between two uses of "had" in if-clauses.
🎯 ISA Priority — Grammar
Section 1 of 5

🕐 Fix 1 — The Time Signal Rule

The flowchart from Unit 6 is working well. One remaining gap: when a sentence has a present time signal (now, today, still, currently), the result clause must use Mixed Conditional form, not Third. The time signal word is the clue — it tells you whether the consequence is happening right now or happened in the past.

🕐 Spot the Time Signal — Then Choose the Result Form
● Present Time Signal → Mixed
nowtodaystillcurrentlyat the momentthis year
Result form: would + base verb
e.g. would be, would still work, would not live
If he had trained harder, he would still be playing professionally now.
● No Time Signal → Third
no "now"no "today"no "still"past event
Result form: would have + past participle
e.g. would have won, would not have been
If she had left earlier, she would have caught the train.
💡 One-Step Check

Before choosing the result form, find the time signal word. Circle it first. If it points to the present (now / today / still) → Mixed → would + base verb. If there is no present signal → Third → would have + past participle.

Section 2 of 5

🔎 Fix 2 — The "had" Test

In Unit 6, you were unsure about Q8: "If Thomas had more time, he would learn to play the guitar." Both B and C contained the word "had" — but they were completely different conditional types. Here is how to tell them apart every time.

The word "had" has two different jobs in English. Look at what comes directly after "had" to know which one it is:

● had + past participle verb → Past Perfect
If she had practised more…
(practised = past participle of "practise")
If they had finished on time…
(finished = past participle of "finish")
Past perfect if-clause → Third or Mixed Conditional
Quick test: can you say "She has practised" or "They have finished"? YES → past participle → past perfect.
● had + noun / adjective → Simple Past of "have"
If he had more time…
("more time" is a noun phrase, not a verb)
If the car had enough fuel…
("enough fuel" is a noun phrase, not a verb)
Simple past if-clause → Second Conditional
Quick test: can you say "He has more time" makes sense as present tense? YES → "had" is simple past of "have" → Second Conditional.
The Test in One Question

Look at the word directly after "had". Is it a past participle verb (something you can do — practised, finished, studied, gone)? YES → past perfect → Third or Mixed. Is it a noun or adjective (more time, enough money, a bigger house)? YES → simple past of "have" → Second Conditional.

Section 3 of 5

📈 Both Rules Together

Here is how the two new rules fit into the full conditional decision process:

📈 Full Decision Flow — Including Both New Rules
Step 1 — "had" test
Is the word after "had" a past participle verb (something you can do)?
NO — noun or adjective
→ Simple past = Second Conditional (would + base verb)
YES — past participle
→ Past perfect → go to Step 2
Step 2 — Time Signal test
Does the sentence contain a present time signal? (now / today / still / currently)
YES — present signal found
→ Mixed Conditional: result = would + base verb
NO — no present signal
→ Third Conditional: result = would have + past participle
BANNED RULE
"will" NEVER appears in any conditional result clause — in any type. If you see it as an option, cross it out immediately.

🎉 All 5 Complete!

Two precision tools added. Both are now part of your conditional flowchart.

The "had" Test

  • Word after "had" = past participle verb → past perfect → Third/Mixed
  • Word after "had" = noun or adjective → simple past of "have" → Second
  • Quick check: "She has [word]" — does it make sense as present perfect? YES → past participle

Time Signal Rule

  • now / today / still / currently → present signal → Mixed
  • Mixed result: would + base verb
  • No present signal → Third → would have + past participle
  • "will" is always wrong in conditional results
