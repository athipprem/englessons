Why this matters 📖

Every notice or short passage you've read so far tells you what you need to know in one or two sentences, right where you'd expect it. Real stories — the kind in books, magazine profiles, and competition reading tests — don't work that way. They spread the important stuff across several paragraphs, and a lot of it is never stated directly at all — you have to notice HOW something is said, not just WHAT is said, to understand how a character feels or what the story is really about. This is a brand-new kind of reading, and today builds it from the very beginning.

Before We Start — a quick warm-up 🔍

Two short examples to get your brain in "story mode" before we build the real tools.

1. "It was raining." Does this tell you WHERE/WHEN a story happens, or WHAT is happening in the story?
2. "She slammed the door and stormed off without a word." What is this sentence mostly showing you?

Good instincts already. Let's turn them into a proper toolkit. Next →

A different kind of passage 🧭

The notices and short passages you've read before give you an answer in one nearby sentence — find the sentence, find the answer. A STORY passage is different: it's several paragraphs long, and the answer to a question might depend on something from paragraph 1 AND paragraph 3 put together. You can't just hunt for one matching sentence — you need to hold the whole story in your head first.

🔑 The new overall strategy

Read the WHOLE passage once, all the way through, before looking at any questions — just to get the general shape of the story (who, roughly what happens, how it ends). THEN go question by question. For each question, ask: is the answer written down directly somewhere (a LITERAL question), or do I need to combine two or more clues myself (an INFERENCE question)?

Setting vs. Plot — two easy-to-mix-up words 🏞️

These two words sound similar in tests because they're both about "what a story has" — but they answer completely different questions.

SETTING
WHERE and WHEN the story happens. A place, a time period, a season, a kind of location.

"a small fishing village, early one foggy morning"
PLOT
WHAT HAPPENS in the story — the events, in order, that make up the action.

"a boy loses his family's only boat and must find a way to earn money to replace it"
🔑 The quick test

Ask: "could I draw this as a still, frozen picture (no movement)?" — if yes, that's SETTING. Ask: "does this describe something changing or happening over time?" — if yes, that's PLOT.

1. "The story takes place in a busy night market lit by paper lanterns." Setting or plot?
2. "A girl discovers a locked box hidden under her grandmother's bed and slowly figures out what's inside." Setting or plot?

Tone — how the writer wants you to FEEL 🎭

Tone is almost never written as a direct sentence like "this part is sad." Instead, the writer picks certain WORDS and DETAILS that make you feel a certain way without saying it outright.

🔑 How to find tone

Look at the word choices, not just the events. "She walked slowly" is neutral. "She trudged, shoulders slumped, staring at the ground" is the SAME event (walking) but the word choices make it feel heavy and sad. Ask yourself: "if I read this out loud, what feeling would be in my voice?"

Read the sentence/paragraph
↓
Which words carry extra feeling, beyond the plain facts?
↓
Name the feeling those specific words point to (proud, worried, hopeful, embarrassed...)
1. "Her hands trembled as she unfolded the exam results, and for a moment she couldn't make herself look down at the page." What tone do these details create?
2. "He grinned the whole walk home, replaying the winning goal over and over in his head." What tone do these details create?

Theme — the big message of the whole story 💡

Theme is the biggest, most zoomed-out idea of all — it's not about any one moment, it's about what the WHOLE story seems to be teaching or valuing, once it's finished.

🔑 How to find theme

Ask yourself at the very end: "What did the main character learn, or what mattered most to them by the end?" Theme is usually a short idea like "don't give up," "family support matters," or "it's okay to ask for help" — never a single fact from one paragraph.

Finish reading the whole story
↓
What changed for the character, or what did they learn?
↓
Turn that into one short, general idea — that's the theme
1. A boy fails to fix his bike three times, almost gives up, but tries a fourth way after his sister offers to help — and it finally works. What's the most likely THEME?

The Kite That Wouldn't Fly

Nok had entered the village kite festival every year since she was six, and every year her kite crashed within seconds of leaving her hands. This year she was determined to finally get one off the ground properly — and stay in the air longer than her cousin Ploy, who won every single time.

She spent two whole weekends building her kite from bamboo strips and old cloth, following instructions from a library book with a cracked spine. The first test flight nose-dived straight into the dirt. She rebuilt the frame. The second attempt spun wildly and tangled itself in the mango tree. Nok sat down hard on the grass, staring at the torn cloth caught in the branches, and didn't say anything for a long time.

Her grandfather, who had been watching from his usual chair on the porch, walked over slowly and picked up one of the broken bamboo strips. "You've got the shape right," he said, turning it over in his hands. "It's the wind you haven't figured out yet — not the kite." He showed her how to check which way the breeze was moving before every launch, using nothing but a handful of dry grass tossed into the air.

Nok rebuilt the kite a third time, this time paying attention to the grass, the direction of the breeze, the angle of her arm. When she let go, the kite wobbled, dipped — and then caught the wind and climbed. She laughed out loud, watching it rise higher than the mango tree, higher than the roofline of her house, a small bright shape steady against the sky.

She didn't beat Ploy that year. But she stood in the field long after the judging ended, kite string looped around her wrist, grinning up at the one thing she had finally gotten to stay in the air.

1. Where does most of this story take place?
2. Which of these is part of the PLOT, not the setting?
3. "Nok sat down hard on the grass, staring at the torn cloth caught in the branches, and didn't say anything for a long time." What tone does this moment create?
4. What did Nok's grandfather teach her that actually fixed the problem?
5. What is the most likely THEME of this story?

Vocabulary keep-sharp 📖

A proper, standalone look at "futile" 🎯

This word has slipped twice now on quick passage mentions — so this time, no passage, just the word on its own with one clear example.

futile = completely useless; having no real chance of success, no matter how much effort goes in.

Example: "Bailing water out of the sinking rowboat with a small cup was futile — the boat was going down no matter what he did."

1. "After the fifth attempt to start the old lawnmower failed, Somchai finally admitted the repair was futile." What does "futile" mean here?
