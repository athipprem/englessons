Why this matters 🌍

Think about a runner in a race. Most runners can sprint hard for the first few seconds — the tricky part is keeping that SAME speed all the way to the finish line, when their legs are tired and it would be easier to slow down. Reading and writing are exactly the same. Anyone can read carefully for the first few sentences of a passage. The real skill — the one that separates a good reader from a great one — is reading the LAST couple of sentences with just as much care as the first ones, even when you're getting tired or want to be done. That's the whole focus of today's lesson.

Before We Start — quick check 🔍

Good news first: your while / meanwhile fix from last time held perfectly on the real test. Try these two warm-ups to prove the core routine is still rock solid:

Warm-up A. "The bell rang. ____, everyone rushed to line up." full stop
Warm-up B. "The soup was too hot to eat, ____ Milo waited patiently for it to cool." comma, mid-sentence

Then, on the very last two gaps, you swapped two words that don't even belong to the same pair — moreover and while. That's not a word-knowledge problem. It's the same thing that trips up tired runners: the care dropped right at the finish. Next →

The gap the routine doesn't cover 🏁

Structure gets you HALFWAY — reading gets you the rest of the way
The punctuation check narrows every gap down to about 3–4 words in the same "family" (all sentence-starters, or all clause-joiners). It can never tell you the job (RESULT vs CONTRAST vs TIME vs ADDITION) — only the actual meaning of the sentence can do that. On Unit 18, gaps 6 AND 7 were BOTH sentence-starters (both came right after a full stop) — so the punctuation step gave the same narrowed-down list for both, and only careful reading could tell them apart. That's exactly where the swap happened.

Notice the pattern didn't fail on a hard word — it failed on the LAST TWO gaps, after five correct answers in a row. Runners call this "leaving something in the tank for the finish." Readers need to do the same thing.

The Finish-Strong habit 🏁NEW HABIT

When you reach the SECOND-TO-LAST gap of any passage: STOP. Take one breath. Re-read the whole sentence out loud in your head — not just skim it — before you decide. Then do the exact same thing for the very last gap.

This isn't a new rule about words. It's a habit about PACING — treating gap 6 and gap 7 with the same seriousness as gap 1, instead of speeding up because you're nearly done.

Worked example: "The bake sale raised more money than ever before. ____, the head teacher announced the total in assembly." — STOP. Full stop before the gap → sentence-starter family. Now read for the job: is this TIME (happening at once), RESULT (caused by), or ADDITION (one more fact)? The announcement HAPPENED BECAUSE of the total being ready → this is RESULT.

Answer: as a result — a sentence-starter, RESULT job. Reading the WHOLE sentence, not just the punctuation, is what got there.

Drill — all four jobs mixed ⚡

Eight sentences, all four families mixed together. Read the WHOLE sentence before choosing — especially the last two, which are deliberately the trickiest.

1. "The choir sang their final note. ____, the dancers stretched backstage, getting ready for their turn." full stop — TIME
2. "The choir sang their final note ____ the dancers stretched backstage, getting ready for their turn." mid-sentence — TIME
3. "The lights dimmed, ____ the crowd gasped." comma — RESULT
4. "The fireworks display was cancelled because of the rain. ____, the town still held a small concert indoors." full stop — CONTRAST
5. "The tickets were completely sold out, ____ extra seats were added at the back." comma — CONTRAST
6. "The storm knocked down several trees in the park. ____, the clean-up crew arrived within the hour." full stop — RESULT
7 FINISH STRONG — "The parade finally reached the town square, and the crowd cheered loudly. ____, confetti rained down from the rooftops." full stop
8 FINISH STRONG — "The mayor began her speech from the podium. ____, the town band tuned their instruments backstage, ready to play the closing song." full stop

Full passage — Sports Day 🏃

One last full passage, five gaps. Read every sentence in full before answering — the last two are marked, but treat every gap the same way.

Sports Day began under a cloudy sky, (1)____ nobody seemed to mind the weather. The younger classes lined up for the sack race (2)____ the older classes stretched by the long-jump pit. The 100m sprint was delayed by ten minutes. (3)____, the timekeeper still managed to record every race accurately. It started to spot with rain just before lunch. (4)____, the teachers moved the final relay indoors. The relay finished in a photo finish, and the camera struggled to catch the exact moment. (5)____, the head teacher had to review the photo twice before announcing a winner.

Gap 1 — "began under a cloudy sky, ____ nobody seemed to mind" comma
Gap 2 — "lined up for the sack race ____ the older classes stretched" mid-sentence, no comma
Gap 3 — "delayed by ten minutes. ____, the timekeeper still managed" full stop
Gap 4 FINISH STRONG — "started to spot with rain. ____, the teachers moved the relay indoors" full stop
Gap 5 FINISH STRONG — "the camera struggled to catch the exact moment. ____, the head teacher had to review the photo twice" full stop
The tree (full stop / comma) still decides the FAMILY.
Reading the whole sentence decides the JOB.
Give gaps 6, 7 (or the last two of ANY passage) the exact same care as gap 1 — that's the whole fix.
