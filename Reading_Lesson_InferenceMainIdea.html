Inference & Main Idea

Reading what the text means, not just what it says.
📚 New Domain — Reading Comprehension · High ISA Priority
Section 1

👁 Literal Retrieval vs Inference: What's the Difference?

There are two types of reading questions. It is important to know which type you are dealing with before you answer.

📑 Literal Retrieval

The answer is directly written in the text. You just need to find it.

Example question: "How long did the expedition last?"

Where to find it: Look for the exact fact in the passage.

The text says: "The expedition lasted six weeks." → Answer: six weeks.

💡 Inference

The answer is NOT written directly. You use clues in the text to work it out.

Example question: "How did the explorer feel about the journey?"

Where to find it: Read the clues — actions, word choices, comparisons — and reason from them.

The text says she "smiled for the first time in months." → Infer: she felt relieved or happy.

The Most Common Mistake

On inference questions, students often pick an answer that is true in real life but not supported by the text. Every inference must be backed by something the passage actually says. If you can't point to a clue in the text, it's not a valid inference.

Section 2

🔍 How to Make an Inference

An inference is not guessing. It is a logical conclusion based on evidence in the text. Think of it like being a detective: the text gives you clues, and you figure out what they mean.

1
Read the question carefully. Does it ask what the text says (literal) or what you can figure out from the text (inference)?
2
Find the relevant part of the passage. Don't answer from memory — go back and re-read the section the question is about.
3
Look for clues. Word choices (positive/negative tone), actions the character takes, what they say or don't say, comparisons, and contrasts all carry meaning.
4
Reason from the clues. Ask: "Given what the text tells me, what is the most logical conclusion?" Eliminate answers that go further than the evidence supports.
The Test for a Valid Inference

Can you point to specific words or sentences in the text that support your answer? If yes → valid inference. If not → you are adding information the text doesn't give you, which is an error.

Section 3

🎯 Finding the Main Idea

The main idea is the most important point the whole passage is making — not just one detail from one sentence.

Main idea vs supporting detail

Supporting details are facts, examples, or evidence that back up the main idea. They are important — but they are not the main point on their own.

Think of it like a tree: the main idea is the trunk. The supporting details are the branches. Remove a branch and the tree still stands. Remove the trunk and everything falls.

How to Find the Main Idea

Ask: "If I had to summarise this whole passage in one sentence, what would I say?" That sentence is the main idea. It should be broad enough to cover the whole passage — not so narrow that it only describes one part of it.

Short Passage
"The town of Millford had been struggling for years. Its main factory had closed in 2015, leaving hundreds without work. Schools were underfunded and young people were leaving for the city. Then, in 2022, a technology company chose Millford as the site for its new regional headquarters. Within two years, over a thousand new jobs had been created, the local school received a generous donation, and property prices had begun to rise again."
1
Read the whole passage. What is it mostly about? — A town that was struggling and then recovered.
2
Identify what the details support. Factory closure, young people leaving, school underfunded = evidence of struggle. New company, jobs, school donation, prices rising = evidence of recovery.
3
Write the main idea: "A struggling town was revived by the arrival of a new technology company." This covers the whole passage.
4
A wrong answer: "The main factory closed in 2015." That's just one detail — too narrow to be the main idea.
✓ Main idea: The arrival of a technology company transformed a town that had been in decline.
Section 4

✍ Author's Purpose

The author's purpose is the reason the writer created the text. There are four main purposes:

📚 INFORM
To give the reader facts, information, or knowledge. Clue: neutral tone, facts and figures, no strong opinion.
🗣 PERSUADE
To convince the reader to believe or do something. Clue: strong language, one-sided argument, calls to action.
🌞 ENTERTAIN
To engage or amuse the reader through a story, humour, or vivid description. Clue: narrative, characters, plot, creative language.
🌟 DESCRIBE
To paint a picture of a person, place, or event in detail. Clue: rich descriptive language, sensory details, imagery.

In reality, many passages mix purposes — but one purpose is usually dominant. For a purpose question, ask: "What is the writer mainly trying to achieve with this text?"

Passage — Read carefully before answering

Cecilia Payne was born in England in 1900. From an early age, she was fascinated by science, but at the time, most universities did not encourage women to study it seriously. Undeterred, she earned a scholarship to Cambridge University, where she studied physics and astronomy with extraordinary dedication.

In 1925, she moved to Harvard University in the United States, where she completed research that would change our understanding of stars forever. She discovered that stars are made mostly of hydrogen and helium — a finding so unexpected that senior scientists at the time refused to believe it. One prominent astronomer even convinced her to downplay the discovery in her published paper.

Years later, when further research confirmed her original conclusion, Cecilia Payne received the recognition she deserved. She became the first woman to be appointed a full professor at Harvard, and her early discovery remains one of the most important in the history of astronomy.

Summary

✅ Key Takeaways

Literal vs Inference

  • Literal: answer is written in the text — just find it
  • Inference: answer must be worked out from clues
  • Every inference must be backed by text evidence
  • Never infer something the text doesn't support

How to Infer

  • Go back and re-read the relevant section
  • Look for clues: word choices, actions, contrasts
  • Ask: what is the most logical conclusion from the clues?
  • Eliminate answers that go beyond the evidence

Main Idea

  • The most important point the whole passage makes
  • Broad enough to cover the whole text
  • Not just one detail or one sentence
  • Summarise the passage in one sentence → that's the main idea

Author's Purpose

  • Inform: facts, neutral tone
  • Persuade: strong language, one-sided argument
  • Entertain: story, characters, narrative
  • Describe: vivid detail, sensory language
