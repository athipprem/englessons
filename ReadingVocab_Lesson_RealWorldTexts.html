Why this matters 🌍

Real life is full of notices with rules that have exceptions: library fines, bus timetables, park signs, permission slips. Getting them wrong isn't just a school problem — mixing up a rule in real life can cost you money or get you in trouble! The skill is reading slowly enough to catch words like "unless," "except," and "responsible for" — because those tiny words often change who a rule actually applies to.

Today's deep-dive: rules with a "who's responsible" chain 🔗

Notices often have a rule, THEN a special case that changes who it applies to. Missing the special case is the single biggest way these questions get missed. The fix is to read every notice in three steps:

1
Find the general rule. Who does WHAT, under normal conditions?
2
Find every exception word. "unless," "except," "if," "under 12," "after," — each one can hand responsibility to someone ELSE.
3
Match the exact situation in the question to the exact exception. Don't stop at the general rule if an exception applies — the exception always wins.

Watch it worked out 👀

📋 Community Tool Shed — Borrowing Rules
Any resident may borrow one tool for up to 3 days. If a tool is returned broken, the borrower must pay for a replacement. If a tool is returned late (after 3 days) but undamaged, the borrower pays a $2 late fee instead. Tools borrowed by children under 12 must be signed out by a parent, who is responsible for any fees.

Question: Jun, age 10, borrows a rake and returns it 5 days later, undamaged. Who pays the late fee?

Step 1 — general rule: a tool returned late but undamaged → the borrower pays a $2 fee.

Step 2 — exception words: "children under 12... must be signed out by a parent, who is responsible for any fees."

Step 3 — match the situation: Jun is 10 (under 12), so the general rule ("the borrower pays") is overridden by the exception — Jun's parent pays the fee, not Jun.

The trap: if you stop reading after the general rule, you'd wrongly answer "Jun." The exception sentence — easy to skim past — is what actually decides the answer.

Same notice, a harder combination: Priya, age 14, borrows a hedge trimmer and returns it broken after 2 days. Who pays, and for what?

Step 1: returned broken → borrower pays for a replacement (not the $2 late fee — it wasn't late, and the "broken" rule applies instead of the "late" rule).

Step 2 — exception check: the parent-responsibility exception only applies to children UNDER 12. Priya is 14.

Step 3: the exception does NOT apply here — Priya herself pays for the replacement.

Practice — Riverside Library Notice 📚

📋 Riverside Library — Borrowing Rules
Books may be borrowed for 14 days. A book returned after 14 days is charged 10 cents per day late, paid by the card-holder. Books borrowed on a Junior Card (ages 6–11) are fine-free if returned within 21 days; after 21 days, the standard 10-cents-per-day fee applies, charged to the parent or guardian who registered the Junior Card.
1. Sam, age 9, borrows a book on his Junior Card and returns it 18 days later. What fee does Sam owe?
2. Priya, age 10, borrows a book on her Junior Card and returns it 25 days later. Who pays the fee, and how much?

Practice — The New Footbridge 🌉

For years, the two halves of Elmswood were separated by the river — anyone walking between them had to cross at the old road bridge, a fifteen-minute detour each way. Parents complained that the walk to school took twice as long as it should, and shopkeepers noticed that customers from the far side rarely visited on foot.

Last spring, the council finally built a footbridge directly across the narrowest point of the river, cutting the walk to under three minutes. Some residents had worried a modern steel bridge would look out of place next to the town's old stone buildings, so the council chose a design with wooden railings and stone-effect posts to match the surrounding architecture.

Since the bridge opened, morning school walks have shortened dramatically, and several shopkeepers on the far side report more customers arriving on foot than ever before.

3. Main idea — What is this passage MAINLY about?
4. Author's purpose — Why does the writer mention that some residents worried a steel bridge would "look out of place"?
