Why this matters 🌍

Think about learning to ride a bike. Even after you can do tricky turns and go fast, if you ever wobble on the very FIRST thing you learned — balancing — everything after it gets shaky too. Last test, a couple of gaps got tripped up on the very first, most basic move in cloze: is this gap a SENTENCE-STARTER or a CLAUSE-JOINER? That one decision comes before anything else, and when it wobbles, the rest wobbles with it. Today isn't about learning anything new — it's about making that very first move so automatic you don't even have to think about it, the same way you don't think about balancing on a bike anymore.

Before We Start — quick check 🔍

Just the punctuation. Don't worry about the exact word yet — only decide the LEVEL.

1. "...the bell rang, ___ everyone lined up by the door." Is the gap a sentence-starter or a clause-joiner?
2. "...the paint had dried overnight. ___, the mural was ready to hang." Is the gap a sentence-starter or a clause-joiner?

This is the whole lesson in miniature — the same one question, over and over, until it's automatic. Next →

The very first move — explained from scratch 🧭

Every cloze gap needs a joining word — a word that connects one idea to another. But before you can pick WHICH word, you have to know WHAT KIND of word the gap needs. There are only two kinds, and the punctuation right before the gap tells you exactly which one, every single time, no exceptions:

Punctuation right before the gapWhat kind of word the gap needs
A full stop — the gap starts a brand new sentenceSentence-starter (however, as a result, meanwhile, moreover)
A comma, or mid-sentence — the gap joins two halves of the SAME sentenceClause-joiner (although, so, while)

That's it. That's the entire first step. You don't even need to read what the sentence MEANS yet — just look at the punctuation immediately before the gap.

What tripped up last time 🔎

On the last test, one gap needed a sentence-starter (full stop right before it), but a clause-joiner word got picked instead — a word that only belongs mid-sentence. That's not a wrong-meaning mistake, it's a skipped-Step-1 mistake: the punctuation check got skipped or rushed. Today's whole job is to make that check so fast and automatic that it happens without even trying.

Rapid drill — LEVEL only, nothing else ⚡

Ten quick sentences. For each one, decide ONLY the level — sentence-starter or clause-joiner. Go fast. Don't think about which exact word fits — that's not today's question.

Now put it together — level, then meaning 🧩

Same two-step order every time: LEVEL first (punctuation), then MEANING (which family fits). Five sentences, full method.

1. "The school garden had barely any rain for two weeks, ___ the vegetable beds started to dry out."
2. "One class watered the tomato plants every morning. ___, another class kept a growth chart on the classroom wall."
3. "The seeds were planted very late in the season, ___ they still produced a good crop by autumn."
4. "___ a sudden hailstorm in early spring, the young seedlings survived without much damage."
5. "The compost bin attracted a lot of insects at first. ___, the students learned this actually helped the soil."

Full passage — same method, gap after gap 🌱

Reminder
Punctuation first, every single gap. Don't skip Step 1, even when you're confident you already know the word.

Level check: locked back in ✅

Full stop before the gap = sentence-starter. Comma or mid-sentence = clause-joiner. Check this FIRST, every single gap, before you even think about which word fits.
