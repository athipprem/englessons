Why this matters 🌍

You've spent a lot of units becoming excellent at conditionals — "if" sentences, Second vs Third, the Will Ban. That work is done and it's solid. But real English grammar tests (and real writing) ask about MORE than just "if" sentences. Today you learn two brand-new rules that have nothing to do with conditionals at all: which verbs can never be "done to" something, and how to tell whether "and" or "but" is the right joining word. These are two completely new tools for your toolbox — first time ever seeing them, so we'll go slowly and from the very beginning.

Before We Start — quick check 🔍

Quick warm-up on your solid conditional skills before we add anything new.

1. "If the museum had been renovated, more visitors would have come." Does the result half break the Will Ban?
2. "If the choir had rehearsed more, the concert would have sounded sharper." Is "had rehearsed" a DOER (no "been") or a GETTER ("been")?

Both correct? Your conditional foundation is exactly where it needs to be. Now let's build on top of it. Next →

New idea #1: some verbs can NEVER have something "done to them" 🎭

First, two brand-new words, explained simply. Look at these two sentences:

SentenceWhat's happening
"The gardener watered the plant."The gardener DOES the action. The plant HAS the action done to it.
"The plant was watered (by the gardener)."Same event, but now the sentence starts with the THING the action was done to.

The first sentence is called active (the subject does the action). The second is called passive (the subject has the action done to it). Most verbs can go either way — "watered" / "was watered" both work. But here's the new rule for today:

🔑 Some verbs work completely ALONE

A verb like "water" needs someone/something to receive the action (you water a PLANT, a LAWN, something). But some verbs never have a receiver at all — the subject just does the thing, by itself, with nothing "done to" anything else. Words like occur, happen, arrive, exist, disappear, matter, seem, belong. Nothing "gets occurred" or "gets happened" — these verbs are SOLO players. Because there's no receiver, you can never turn them into passive voice — "was occurred" or "is happened" isn't real English, no matter how you phrase it.

SOLO verbs — active ONLY, passive is impossibleTEAM verbs — can be active OR passive
Earthquakes occur along fault lines.The workers repaired the fault line. / The fault line was repaired.
The accident happened at noon.The city recorded the accident. / The accident was recorded.
The bus arrived late.The driver delayed the bus. / The bus was delayed.
The ancient bridge still exists.Engineers maintain the bridge. / The bridge is maintained.
The 3-second test: try adding "...by someone/something" right after the verb.
Makes sense → TEAM verb, passive is fine.
Sounds silly → SOLO verb, passive is impossible, active only.

Quick check — say the sentence with "by someone" added. Does it sound normal, or silly?

1. "The storm occurred quickly." → "The storm occurred quickly BY SOMETHING."
2. "The teacher decorated the classroom." → "The classroom was decorated BY the teacher."
3. "The missing dog disappeared overnight." → "The missing dog disappeared overnight BY SOMETHING."

Watch it worked out — spotting a SOLO verb forced into passive 👀

Sentence: "Chromium and nickel are occurred commonly in areas which are also rich in magnesium."

Step 1 — find the verb: "occurred". Try the by-someone test: "are occurred by something"? That sounds completely wrong — nothing DOES the occurring to chromium and nickel, they just occur, on their own.

Step 2 — SOLO verb confirmed: "occur" is a SOLO verb, just like happen/arrive/exist. It can NEVER be passive, in any tense.

Step 3 — fix it: the correct form is simply the active, plain present tense: "Chromium and nickel occur commonly in areas which are also rich in magnesium." (Note: "are" by itself, describing what the areas ARE, is fine — that's not a passive verb, it's just the normal verb "to be".)

The lesson: "was/were/is/are + verb-with-ed-or-en-ending" isn't automatically wrong — but if the base verb is a SOLO verb (occur, happen, arrive, exist, disappear, matter, seem, belong), that passive shape is never correct, no matter how official-sounding the sentence looks.

New idea #2: is it an "and" idea, or a "but" idea? 🔀

You already know although/however signal a surprise or contrast, and so/as-a-result signal a cause. "And" and "but" work the exact same way, just in a single sentence instead of joining two full sentences:

AND — the two ideas AGREE / ADD TOGETHERBUT — the two ideas CONTRAST / SURPRISE you
Maya trained every day and won first place. (hard work → good result — matches, no surprise)Maya trained every day but still came last. (hard work → you'd expect a good result, but she didn't get one — a surprise)
The shop was closed and the lights were off. (two facts about the same situation — matches)The shop was closed but the lights were still on. (you'd expect lights off too — a surprise)
Read the TWO ideas being joined. Do they point the SAME direction (both good, both bad, or simply adding on)? → and. Does the SECOND idea surprise you, given the FIRST? → but.

In "which underlined part is wrong?" questions, the mistake is sometimes NOT a verb at all — it's the wrong joining word. A sentence using "and" for two contrasting ideas (or "but" for two matching ideas) is the error, even if every verb in it is perfectly fine.

Quick check — do the two ideas agree, or contrast?

1. "Craig was always quick at maths, ___ he had trouble with reading and writing."
2. "The rain poured all afternoon, ___ the football match was cancelled."
3. "The bakery ran out of bread by 9am, ___ still had plenty of cakes left."

The real thing — find the wrong underlined part (A)(B)(C)(D) 🧩

Each sentence below is split into four labelled parts. ONE part contains the error. It could be any of the checks you now know: Will Ban, a broken verb form, mismatched halves, a SOLO verb forced into passive, or the wrong and/but. Read the whole sentence first, then choose the part.

🚫 Which underlined part is grammatically wrong?
"(A) The volcano (B) had been dormant for years, (C) and (D) scientists were caught off guard when it suddenly erupted."
🚫 Which underlined part is grammatically wrong?
"(A) A total eclipse (B) are occurred (C) only when the moon (D) passes directly between the sun and Earth."
🚫 Which underlined part is grammatically wrong?
"(A) If the ferry engine (B) had been serviced sooner, (C) the crossing (D) will not have been delayed."
🚫 Which underlined part is grammatically wrong?
"(A) The twins (B) look almost identical, (C) but (D) they also share the exact same taste in music."
Two new checks join your toolbox today: SOLO verbs (occur, happen, arrive, exist, disappear, matter, seem, belong) can never be passive — active only. And "and" needs two matching/adding ideas, while "but" needs two contrasting/surprising ones. Same careful read-the-whole-sentence habit as always — just two more things to check for.
