Unit 1 · Phase 1: Foundation Hardening

Vocabulary in Context

Stop guessing words in isolation. Use the passage, connotation, and word families to find the precise answer.
⚠ Vocabulary Gap — ASMOPSS: Q8 ✗ Intermediate, Q9 ✗ Applied (connotation & nuance)
Section 1

📖 Read the Context, Not Just the Word

The biggest vocabulary mistake is trying to remember what a word means in isolation — on its own, out of context. In a real passage, the surrounding sentences always tell you what a word means, even if you have never seen it before.

The Core Rule

Never skip an unknown word and guess. Instead, read the sentences before and after it. The passage is giving you the definition — you just need to spot it.

Look at this example:

"The desert is an arid environment. Rain rarely falls there, and the soil is too dry for most plants to survive."

Even if you don't know the word arid, the sentences after it tell you: rain rarely falls, soil is too dry. → Arid means very dry.

Section 2

🔍 Four Types of Context Clues

Writers give you clues in four main ways. Learn to spot which type is in the passage.

1. Definition Clue
The author explains the word directly, often using "is", "means", or a comma.
"A palaeontologist, a scientist who studies fossils, found a new dinosaur species."
2. Example Clue
Examples are given to illustrate the meaning. Look for "such as", "for example", "like".
"The island had many indigenous animals, such as the kiwi bird and the tuatara lizard, found nowhere else on Earth."
3. Contrast Clue
The opposite meaning is given. Look for "but", "however", "unlike", "although".
"The speaker was eloquent, but her assistant could barely string two sentences together."
4. Inference Clue
No direct clue — you piece together the meaning from the overall situation.
"After the long race, the runners were fatigued. They sat down, drank water, and could barely move."
Inference Clues Need the Whole Passage

For inference clues, read at least two sentences before and after the unknown word. The clue is scattered — not in one place.

Section 3

🌟 Connotation — The Feeling Behind the Word

Two words can have almost the same dictionary meaning (denotation) but very different feelings attached to them (connotation). ISA vocabulary questions often test whether you can feel this difference.

Example: Words for "thin"

🔴 Negative
scrawny
Thin in an unhealthy, unappealing way
⚪ Neutral
thin
Simply describes body shape — no judgement
🟢 Positive
slender
Thin in an elegant, graceful way

Example: Words for "determined"

🔴 Negative
stubborn
Refuses to change; annoyingly fixed
⚪ Neutral
firm
Steady in a decision — no strong feeling
🟢 Positive
resolute
Strong-willed in an admirable way
How to spot connotation in a question

Look at the tone of the surrounding passage. Is the author praising, criticising, or being neutral? That tells you whether the gap needs a positive, negative, or neutral word.

Section 4

🏠 Word Families — One Root, Many Forms

Many English words belong to the same family — they share a root but change form depending on how they are used in a sentence. Knowing a word's family dramatically increases your vocabulary.

How to read a word family table:

Root / BaseNounVerbAdjectiveAdverb
discover discovery discover / discovered discoverable —
courage courage / encouragement encourage courageous courageously
possible possibility — possible / impossible possibly
vary variety / variation vary various / variable variously
precise precision — precise / imprecise precisely
The Word Family Test

When you see an unknown word, strip away the ending (-tion, -ous, -ly, -ment, -able) and see if you recognise the root. Then rebuild the meaning from there.

Section 5

🎯 Near Synonyms — Almost Right vs Exactly Right

ISA vocabulary questions often give you four words that all mean roughly the same thing. Your job is to find the one that fits precisely in the context. This is what closes Q8 and Q9 type errors.

Example: Words for "walked quickly"

strode
Walked with long, confident steps — fits a determined character
marched
Walked in a stiff, military way — fits a soldier or protest
scurried
Moved quickly in a small, nervous way — fits a small animal or anxious person
sprinted
Ran at full speed — not walking

If the passage says: "The general strode into the room, his medals gleaming." — you can't replace strode with scurried (too timid) or sprinted (wrong action). Only strode or marched would fit — and only one fits the character.

The Near Synonym Trap

All four choices will seem correct if you only look at the word. Always check: does the full passage support this exact choice? The passage is more important than your memory of the word.

Lesson Summary

🏆 What to Remember

Context Clue Types

  • Definition — explained directly
  • Example — "such as", "for example"
  • Contrast — "but", "however", "unlike"
  • Inference — piece it together

Connotation

  • Negative / Neutral / Positive feeling
  • Match the tone of the passage
  • e.g. scrawny ✗ vs slender ✓ (positive text)
  • stubborn ✗ vs resolute ✓ (praise text)

Word Families

  • Strip the ending to find the root
  • Rebuild: noun / verb / adjective / adverb
  • e.g. courage → courageous → courageously

Near Synonyms

  • All choices look similar — that's the trap
  • The passage tells you which is exactly right
  • Check: who is doing what, and how?
  • e.g. crept ≠ marched ≠ charged
