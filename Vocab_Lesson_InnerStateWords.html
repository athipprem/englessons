Unit 7  ·  Vocabulary

Inner-State Words

How a character feels — not what they do, not what they are. Match the state, not the quality.
🎯 ISA Priority — Vocabulary Inference
Section 1 of 5

🧠 Three Types of Words — Only One Describes a Feeling

In Unit 6, both vocabulary errors were about the same thing: choosing a word that described what a character did or what quality they had — instead of the word that described how they felt inside. These are very different things, and a passage will give you different clues for each type.

▶ Action Words
What a character DOES
practised, trained, worked, stayed, helped, ran, built
Clue: verbs — describes behaviour and effort
⭐ Quality Words
What a character IS or HAS
talented, organised, careful, experienced, confident, skilled
Clue: adjectives — describes personality/ability
💕 Inner-State Words
How a character FEELS inside
apprehensive, assured, restless, elated, overwhelmed, uneasy, serene
Clue: emotional/psychological descriptions — body language, reactions, internal feelings
The Trap

A passage can describe someone who is both talented (quality) AND nervous (state). If the underlined word is an inner-state word, the correct answer must match how they feel — not how good they are. Choosing a quality word when the question is about a state word is the most common error at this level.

Section 2 of 5

🔍 How Passages Signal Inner States

Writers do not always say "she felt scared" directly. They show inner states through four types of clue. Learn to recognise all four:

🔌 Body Language Clues
"gave a small nod", "shoulders dropped", "hands trembling", "stood very still"
→ Physical reactions reveal inner feelings. Trembling = nervous. Small nod = calm/settled.
🙄 Facial / Expression Clues
"looked up with wide eyes", "froze", "jaw tightened", "face broke into a smile"
→ Facial reactions show surprise, fear, relief, joy. Always ask: what would make a face do that?
🖼️ Direct Statement Clues
"he could not sleep the night before", "she kept checking the clock", "he felt a knot in his stomach"
→ The passage tells you directly what the character experienced. Trust these completely.
💡 Context / Situation Clues
"practised every evening for two weeks before a competition" = motivated by worry, not just enthusiasm
→ Intense preparation before a high-stakes event often signals anxiety or pressure, not confidence.
💡 The State-Match Rule

When the underlined word is an inner-state word, ask: "What is the character experiencing inside — not what are they doing, not what qualities do they have?" Find the clue that describes the feeling, and match it to the answer option that names that feeling precisely.

Section 3 of 5

📝 Worked Example — Two Inner-State Words

Here is the passage from Unit 6 that contained the two errors, with the analysis shown step by step:

Twelve-year-old Nico had been (16) apprehensive about entering the junior cooking contest, but he had practised his recipe every evening for two weeks. He stood at his stall and began to work with quiet (17) precision. As the timer wound down, Nico tasted his dish and gave a small nod. He felt (19) assured that he had done his best work.
(16) apprehensive
Context clue: "practised his recipe every evening for two weeks" before a contest = intensive preparation driven by worry about the outcome.
Near-synonym trap: Option A was "confident and well-prepared." He WAS well-prepared — true. But "apprehensive" is a STATE word (how he felt), not a quality. Prepared = what he did. Apprehensive = how he felt about it.
✓ Correct: nervous and worried — the inner feeling, not the preparation behaviour.
(19) assured
Body language clue: "gave a small nod" = a calm, quiet, settled gesture — not the action of someone who is anxious or tense. Direct statement: "felt assured that he had done his best work" = inner certainty.
Near-synonym trap: Option C was "anxious but determined." He might be determined — but the "small nod" is a CALM gesture. Anxious people do not give quiet, settled nods; they fidget, hesitate, or freeze. The physical clue eliminates C.
✓ Correct: calm and confident — the body language clue and the direct statement both point here.

🎉 All 5 Complete!

Match the feeling, not the quality. The passage always gives you the clue — find it and trust it.

The State-Match Rule

  • Inner-state words = how they FEEL, not what they DO
  • Find the specific clue: body language, face, direct statement, context
  • Ask: what feeling does this clue point to — not what quality

Eliminate the Trap Option

  • Both options may be positive/plausible — only one matches the clue
  • Actions ≠ states: "practised hard" does not mean "confident"
  • Qualities ≠ states: "determined" is not the same as "assured"
  • Trust the specific passage evidence over general impressions
