Why this matters 🌍

If a friend texts you "I guess I'll come to the party" instead of "Yes! I'll be there!", you instantly know something — even though they never said "I don't really want to go." You figured it out from HOW they said it, not from a direct statement. That's inference: using clues to work out something the text never says outright. ASMOPSS and ISA reading passages test this constantly — the most important idea is often never written in one single sentence.

Before We Start — quick check 🔍

For each question below, is the answer written word-for-word in the text, or would you have to figure it out from clues?

1. A notice says: "Library Junior Cards expire on the member's 12th birthday." Question: "How old can you be to hold a Junior Card?"
2. A story says a boy "kept glancing at the clock and tapping his pencil" during class. Question: "How does the boy most likely feel?"

Last unit's vocab held near-perfect — 5/6 — with one miss: "reluctant" (means unwilling/hesitant), where the answer chosen was "proud and confident" — almost the opposite feeling. Today we add one check that stops that from happening again. Next →

What inference actually is 🔎

An inference is your BEST explanation that fits every clue the text gives you — not a wild guess, and not something copied word-for-word either.

Read the question
↓
Is the exact answer written, word-for-word, somewhere in the text?
YES
Direct fact — find it, copy it.
NO ↓
Gather the clues
1
Find 2–3 separate clues in the text — small details, actions, or word choices.
2
Ask: what do these clues have IN COMMON? What's really going on underneath them?
3
Pick the answer option that fits ALL the clues, not just one of them — and check it doesn't contradict any clue.
Inference = the best explanation that fits every clue. Never pick an answer that ignores a clue, and never pick one that isn't supported by ANY clue.

Worked example — Rooftop Beehives 🐝

Last spring, Ridgeway School installed four beehives on its flat roof, hoping to help pollinate the vegetable garden two floors below. When the boxes first arrived, several students hung back near the stairwell door instead of crossing the roof, and a few asked the caretaker whether the bees could reach the playground. A local beekeeper visited the following week, opened one hive slowly, and let students watch from a safe distance as she checked the honeycomb frames. By the end of her visit, a small crowd had gathered right beside the hives, and two students asked if they could help with the next inspection. Within a month, the vegetable garden's pumpkin vines were covered in more flowers than any previous year.

1. What can you infer about how the students felt about the bees BEFORE the beekeeper's visit?

How to reason it out: Clue 1 — "several students hung back... instead of crossing the roof." Clue 2 — "a few asked... whether the bees could reach the playground." Neither clue says the word "nervous," but hanging back AND asking a safety question both point the same direction — some caution about the bees. That's option B: not written directly, but the best fit for both clues, and no clue says excited, uninterested, or angry.

2. What is the passage mainly suggesting happened by the end?

Vocabulary — the connotation check 🔤

🚫 The trap: judging a word by its "feeling," not its clue

Some words can be mistaken for their near-OPPOSITE if you only skim the sentence's general mood instead of reading the exact clue. Being nervous about something new and being excited about something new can both happen in the SAME kind of situation — a first day, a first attempt, a first dive — so the word itself has to be checked against its own sentence, not against the general vibe of the story.

✅ THE CHECK: quote the exact clue phrase. Does your chosen meaning MATCH that phrase, or does it FIGHT it? If it fights it, even a little, it's wrong — no matter how the rest of the story feels.

Try it — three words, three clues:

"Priya stood at the edge of the diving board, wary of the height, and took a full minute before she finally jumped." What does "wary" mean here?
"After losing the match three times in a row, Sam grew resigned to defeat and stopped even trying new tactics." What does "resigned" mean here?
"The shopkeeper seemed indifferent to whether Leo bought the bicycle or not, barely looking up from her newspaper." What does "indifferent" mean here?

Practice — New Recycling Bins 🗑️

📋 NOTICE — New Recycling Bins
Starting Monday, the three-colour recycling bins outside the canteen will only accept CLEAN, DRY items. Any bag found containing food waste or liquids will be removed by staff and left beside the bins with a red tag, and the class responsible will be asked to sort it properly before it can be collected. Bins are checked twice daily. Students who consistently follow the new rules will be invited to join the Green Team, which chooses new recycling posters for the school each term.
1. If a bag with food waste is found, what happens to it?
2. What can you infer is the PURPOSE of inviting students to the Green Team?
3. "Leo felt vindicated when his class's careful sorting earned them a Green Team invitation after weeks of extra effort." What does "vindicated" mean here?
Inference: gather the clues, find what they share, pick the explanation that fits all of them. Vocabulary: quote the exact clue, check your meaning MATCHES it — never judge a word by the story's overall mood.
