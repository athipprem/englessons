Why this matters 📋

Official notices, school rules, competition rules, and club announcements are full of sentences like "the rules require that every entrant sign the form." Getting this pattern automatic isn't just for tests — it's exactly how real notices are written. Today we fix the ONE thing that tripped it up last time.

🔎 What actually happened last test

Look closely at your three subjunctive misses last unit: you picked "displays," "explains," and "becomes" — every single time, the ordinary form that matches the subject. That's not confusion about the rule — you clearly understand it, because you've never once picked a random wrong word. Your ear is just VERY well trained at ordinary sentences, so well trained that it's now overruling a brand-new rule before you even get a chance to think about it. Today's fix isn't re-explaining the rule — it's teaching your EYES to spot the trigger word before your EARS get a vote.

Quick recap — the rule itself 📢

You already know this part correctly, so this is just a reminder before we fix the real issue.

After recommend that / suggest that / insist that / require that / demand that — the verb goes back to its BASE FORM. No -s. No "to." No tense change. Every time, no matter the subject.

Example: "The doctor recommended that he drink more water" — not "drinks," even though "he" would normally take "drinks" in an ordinary sentence.

The new habit — trigger word FIRST 🔍

From now on, before you even look at the verb options, do this one extra step:

Read the sentence. Is there a trigger word — recommend / suggest / insist / require / demand — sitting right before "that"?
YES — spot it first
Go STRAIGHT to the base form. Ignore what the subject would normally take.
NO trigger word
Use the normal tense as usual — the subject decides, like always.
🔑 Why "spot it first" fixes the problem

If you read the whole sentence and just ask "what sounds right?", your ear answers first — and your ear is trained on thousands of ORDINARY sentences, not this rare exception. But if you train yourself to scan for the trigger word BEFORE you even think about the verb, you get to make the decision with your eyes, on purpose, instead of letting your ear guess for you.
