Writers say things they don't mean — on purpose

Good writers paint pictures with words. They compare things, or give feelings to objects, so you feel what they describe. These pictures are called figures of speech. Your job as a reader: spot them, then ask "what does this really mean?"

The four you must know 🎨

FigureWhat it doesExample
SimileCompares two things using like or as"She ran like the wind." "As brave as a lion."
MetaphorSays one thing IS another (no "like"/"as")"The classroom was a zoo." "Time is a thief."
PersonificationGives human actions to a thing or animal"The wind whispered." "The sea swallowed the beach."
IdiomA saying whose words don't mean what they say"It's raining cats and dogs." "Piece of cake."
The reader's two questions

1. Which figure is it? (Is there a "like"/"as"? Is a thing doing a human action?)
2. What does it really mean? Turn the picture into plain words.

Remember the Milestone
"Moving the lighthouse felt like moving a family grave." There were no real graves. The simile showed how deeply the local people's feelings were tied to the place. That is reading a figure for its meaning — exactly the skill below.
