Section 1

🕐 The Three Conditionals

A conditional sentence talks about a condition and its result. It has two parts: the if-clause (the condition) and the main clause (the result).

There are three types, and each one is used for a different situation:

Zero Conditional
Facts — always true
If + present simple, present simple
If you heat ice, it melts.
If plants don't get water, they die.
First Conditional
Real future — possible situation
If + present simple, will + base verb
If it rains tomorrow, we will stay inside.
If she studies hard, she will pass.
Second Conditional
Unreal / imaginary — not likely now
If + past simple, would + base verb
If I had wings, I would fly to school.
If she were taller, she would play basketball.
The Key Difference

Zero = always true (science, facts). First = possible future (realistic). Second = imaginary (not real now, uses past tense in the if-clause + would in the result).

Section 2

📝 The Formulas Side by Side

Learn to recognise which type you are looking at from the tense of the verbs.

TypeIf-clause verbMain clause verbQuick signal
Zero present simple (heats, rains, is) present simple (melts, floods, are) General truth / science fact
First present simple (studies, leaves, wins) will + base verb (will pass, will miss, will win) Realistic future possibility
Second past simple (had, were, studied) would + base verb (would go, would be, would study) Imaginary / unlikely now
Most Common Error

Writing "If I would have…" — this is wrong. The if-clause never takes would. If I had a car, I would drive to school. ✓  |  If I would have a car, I would drive. ✗

Watch out for "were" vs "was"

In formal written English and in ISA, the second conditional uses were for all subjects (not just plural):

✓ If she were the captain, she would change the team.
✗ If she was the captain, she would change the team. (less formal — avoid in ISA writing)
Section 3

⏱ Tense Consistency in Paragraphs

When you write a paragraph, the tense must stay consistent unless you have a good reason to change it (e.g. switching from past events to a present reflection).

Tense Slip — Common in Writing

Starting in past tense and accidentally switching to present: "The boy ran to the park and plays with his friends." → should be "ran…and played".

The Two-Tense Paragraphs Test

When editing your writing, underline every verb and check that they all belong to the same time zone:

Past paragraph (correct): The explorers arrived at the cave. They lit their torches and stepped inside carefully. The air inside was cold and damp.

Mixed tenses (incorrect): The explorers arrived at the cave. They light their torches and step inside. The air inside was cold.
Section 4

🔗 Clause Embedding — Joining Ideas with Who, Which, That, When

An embedded clause adds extra information inside a sentence. It makes your writing more sophisticated and varied.

Instead of two short sentences: "The scientist made a discovery. She had worked for ten years."
Write one embedded sentence: "The scientist, who had worked for ten years, made an important discovery."

Which Word to Use?

who Use for people. → "The teacher who helped me is very kind."
which Use for things or animals. → "The book, which was very old, fell apart when I opened it."
that Use for people or things in defining clauses. → "The car that she drives is red."
when Use for time. → "I remember the day when we first arrived in the city."
Commas Rule

If the embedded clause is EXTRA information (you could remove it and the sentence still makes sense), use commas. If it's ESSENTIAL (defines exactly which one), no commas needed.
Extra: "My sister, who lives in London, called me." (I only have one sister — the clause is extra info.)
Essential: "The student who studied hardest won the prize." (No commas — it tells us exactly which student.)

Lesson Summary

🏆 What to Remember

Zero Conditional

  • Facts — always true
  • If + present, present
  • "If ice melts, it becomes water."

First Conditional

  • Real future possibility
  • If + present, will + base
  • "If it rains, I will stay home."

Second Conditional

  • Imaginary / not real now
  • If + past, would + base
  • Use "were" not "was" formally

Clause Embedding

  • who → people
  • which → things (with commas if extra)
  • that → essential defining clauses
  • when → time references
