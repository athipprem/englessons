Before We Start — You Scored 6/6 on Vocabulary 🎯

In Unit 8, all six vocabulary questions were correct. That is perfect. But there is a problem: the clues in Unit 8 were placed right next to the target word. ISA tests hide clues further away — one or two sentences before the word, and the wrong options are designed to confuse. This lesson teaches you how to handle that.

Quick Check 1 — Easy vs Hard

Read the two sentences below. Which one has the clue hidden further from the target word?

Sentence A: "The room was spotless — every surface gleamed. Her tidiness was meticulous."
Sentence B: "The room smelled of paint. The window was open. Her work was meticulous, precise, and thorough."

Quick Check 2 — Spotting a Distractor

Read this sentence: "The athlete trained through every injury, setback, and loss. Her resilience was what coaches noticed most."

Which option is the distractor — the one that sounds connected to the topic but is not supported by the clue?

What changes in this lesson

⚠ Two new challenges from Unit 9 onwards:
1. Clues are further away — the clue might be in the sentence before the target word, or even two sentences before. You cannot just read the phrase around the word. You must read back.

2. Distractors are smarter — one wrong option will feel connected to the topic ("athlete" → "strength") but it is not actually stated in the passage. Only the clue counts. Your feeling about the topic does not count.
The Quote and Match method still works.
It just requires you to look further back in the passage.

The Quote and Match Method — 3 Steps 🔍

This is the complete method for every vocabulary question in every ISA passage. Follow all three steps every time.

QUOTE AND MATCH — 3 Steps
1
Find the target word in the passage
Circle or underline it. Do not guess its meaning from the options yet — that pulls you toward traps. Find the word first, then look at what surrounds it.
2
Quote the clue — in that sentence AND the sentence before
The clue is always somewhere nearby. Read the sentence with the target word. If that is not enough, read the sentence before it. When you find words that explain what the target word means, quote them exactly in your head: "The clue says '___'". Never quote your own idea — only what is in the text.
3
Match the clue to each option — test them one by one
Go through A, B, C, D. For each one, ask: "Is this what the clue actually said?" — not "does this feel right for this topic?" One option will match your quoted clue exactly. The others will either contradict it or simply not be mentioned in the passage at all.
⚠ The distractor trap — always check this
The hardest wrong option is one that fits the general topic but is not said in the passage. For example: a passage about a marathon runner using the word "endurance" — option A might be "speed" (fits athletes, but not stated) and option B might be "ability to last a long time" (what the passage actually says). Your clue quote rules out A immediately: the passage never mentioned speed.

Where to look for the clue

Clue locationHow to find itExample
Same sentenceRead normally — clue is in the same phrase"The room was immaculate — not a speck of dust anywhere."
Sentence beforeRead the sentence before the target word"She had scrubbed every surface for hours. Her work was immaculate."
Two sentences beforeRead back two sentences if needed"The event was a disaster. Nothing went according to plan. Looking back, the decisions seemed rash."

At ISA level, the clue is almost always within two sentences of the target word. If you cannot find it, read the sentence before again. Do not guess.

Worked Examples — Harder Clues 💡

Watch how the 3-step method works when the clue is not right next to the word.

Worked Example 1 — Clue one sentence before
Mini Passage
Maya had barely spoken since the announcement. She sat in the corner of the room, arms folded, staring at the wall. Her mood was sullen for the rest of the afternoon.

Question: The word "sullen" means most nearly —

Step 1 — Find the word: "sullen" is in the last sentence.

STEP 2Quote the clue. "sullen" is not explained in its own sentence. Read the sentence before: "She sat in the corner of the room, arms folded, staring at the wall." And the sentence before that: "Maya had barely spoken since the announcement." Two clues: barely spoken + arms folded, staring at wall. Together they describe someone who is withdrawn and in a bad mood.
STEP 3Match. A: cheerful (opposite — ruled out). B: quiet and bad-tempered (matches "barely spoken" + folded arms). C: tired (not mentioned — passage says mood, not energy). D: nervous (possible topic-feel, but passage says no words or signs of nerves). Answer: B
Worked Example 2 — Distractor check
Mini Passage
The explorer had crossed three mountain ranges in six days without stopping to rest. She had run out of food on the fourth day. Despite this, she pressed on, never once turning back. Her friends called her intrepid.

Question: The word "intrepid" means most nearly —

STEP 2Quote the clue. The sentence with "intrepid" says her friends called her this, but does not explain it. Read the sentences before: "pressed on, never once turning back" and "ran out of food…despite this, she pressed on." Clue = kept going despite danger and hardship.
STEP 3Match + distractor check. A: physically very strong (topic-feel distractor — "three mountain ranges" suggests strength, but the passage says she kept going, not that she was physically powerful). B: fearless and willing to face difficulty (matches "pressed on, never turning back" despite danger). C: intelligent (not mentioned anywhere). D: experienced at climbing (topic-feel distractor — the passage mentions mountains but never says she was experienced). Answer: B

Key lesson: A and D both feel connected to "mountain explorer" but neither is in the clue. Your quote ("pressed on, never turning back") points to B only.

Apply the 3-step method. For each passage: find the word, quote the clue, match one by one. The distractors are designed to feel right — use your quote, not your feeling.

Summary — What to Remember 📋

Step 1: Find the word
Step 2: Quote the clue (sentence before if needed)
Step 3: Match each option to the clue — not your feeling
The methodWhy it matters at ISA level
Step 2 — look back one sentenceISA clues are not always in the same sentence as the word. If the same sentence does not help, the sentence before almost always does.
Step 3 — test every optionDo not stop at the first option that feels right. Test all four. The distractor is designed to feel right. Your quoted clue will rule it out.
Distractor ruleIf an option connects to the topic but is not in the clue, eliminate it. Topic-feel does not count. Only the clue counts.
⚠ The one mistake that costs the most marks:
Choosing an option because it "sounds like" what the passage is about, without quoting a specific clue to support it. Your quote is your evidence. No quote = no answer.

Read this sentence: "The musician had not eaten, had barely slept, and had skipped every practice session for a week. His playing had become erratic."

In the box below, write the clue you would quote for "erratic" and the meaning you would choose.

✅ Well done for trying! Here is the model answer:

Clue to quote: "had not eaten, had barely slept, and had skipped every practice session" — these are all signs of something going wrong and becoming unreliable.

Meaning of "erratic": unpredictable or unreliable — happening in an irregular, disorganised way. Not following a steady pattern.

Distractor check: "bad" (too vague — not supported by a specific clue). "Quiet" (not mentioned). "Slow" (not mentioned). Only "unpredictable / irregular" matches what the clues say.
