Warm-up 4 — the one that got away (Unit 9, Q14)

You were asked to find the sentence with a grammar error. You chose:

"If the bridge had a stronger support structure, it would carry heavier loads safely."

But this sentence is CORRECT. Why?

What Unit 9 Q14 teaches us

You saw "had + a stronger support structure" and your alarm bell rang — "had + noun, suspicious!" But the Had Test never says had + noun is WRONG. It says had + noun = Second if-clause. Whether the sentence is correct depends on the second half: does the result clause match?

One half of a sentence is never an error.
Errors live in the MATCH between the two halves.

The Cross-Check 🔗 — classify BOTH halves

You now classify if-clauses instantly. The next level: error-spotting questions where every sentence must be checked twice — once for each half. A sentence is only wrong when the two halves disagree.

If-clause says…Result clause must say…Example result
2nd (had + noun/adj, or simple past)would + base verbwould carry, would visit, would feel
3rd (had + past participle)would have + past participlewould have carried, would have won
3rd if + time word like "today" / "now"would + base verb (Mixed)would be … today
2nd if-clause✗ would have + past participleMISMATCH — the Unit 9 trap
any if-clause✗ will / will haveWILL BAN — always wrong
The pencil habit for error-spotting questions
Above each sentence, write two tiny numbers: the if-clause type and the result type. "2 | 2" = match, move on. "3 | 3" = match, move on. "2 | 3" = found it. Never judge a sentence by one half alone — that is exactly how Q14 got away.
Worked example

"If the bakery had a bigger oven, it would have doubled its morning orders."
Half 1: "had a bigger oven" → had + noun → 2nd.
Half 2: "would have doubled" → would have + p.p. → 3rd.
Write it: 2 | 3 → MISMATCH. Found the error — and no correct sentence got blamed.
