Part 1 · Reading — Figures of speech 🎨

Writers compare things, or give feelings to objects, so you feel what they describe. These pictures are figures of speech. Spot them, then ask "what does this really mean?"

FigureWhat it doesExample
SimileCompares using like or as"ran like the wind"; "as brave as a lion"
MetaphorSays one thing IS another"the classroom was a zoo"
PersonificationGives human actions to a thing"the wind whispered"
IdiomA saying whose words don't mean what they say"piece of cake"
Remember the Milestone
"Moving the lighthouse felt like moving a family grave." No real graves — the simile showed how deeply the local people's feelings were tied to the place. That is reading a figure for its meaning.

Part 2 · Vocabulary — The Prove-It move ✅

At the Milestone you scored 5/6 — only "elated" slipped, because the story pulled you away from the quote. The right answer always sits in the words next to the target word. Your meaning must be something the quote actually shows.

TrapWhat it does
Word-feelThe word sounds like another word (gruelling → "gruesome", dank → "dark").
Story-feelAn option fits the story — but the quote doesn't prove it (the brother was wrong earlier, so "surprised" feels right).
Worked example — "elated"
"…she was elated, leaping so high that her badge flew off."

Underline the quote: leaping so high her badge flew off, then tick each option against it.
• "extremely happy" → leaping for joy ✅
• "surprised her brother was wrong" → quote says nothing about the brother ❌ story-feel

If the quote doesn't prove it, it isn't the answer.
